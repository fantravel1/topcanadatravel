About Capilano Suspension Bridge

The Capilano Suspension Bridge is one of Vancouver's most iconic attractions, drawing visitors from around the world to experience the thrill of crossing 137 metres above the Capilano River through ancient coastal rainforest. What began as a simple hemp rope bridge in 1889 has evolved into a world-class nature park.

Scottish civil engineer George Grant Mackay built the original bridge to access his property on the far side of the canyon. Made of hemp rope and cedar planks, it was rebuilt in 1903 with wire cable. The current bridge, reconstructed in 1956, can support 96 full-grown elephants - or 1,300 people - at once.

Beyond the famous bridge, the park offers multiple attractions including the cliff-hugging Cliffwalk, the suspended Treetops Adventure, nature trails through old-growth forest, and educational exhibits about the region's natural and cultural history. The park encompasses 27 acres of towering Douglas firs, Western red cedars, and hemlock trees, some over 800 years old.

Engineering Marvel

The suspension bridge is anchored by 13 tons of concrete on each side and features steel cables that could stretch from Vancouver to Whistler if laid end to end. The bridge sways naturally as people cross - this flexibility is actually what makes it so strong and safe.

Park Attractions

The Suspension Bridge

The star attraction: 137 metres long, suspended 70 metres above the Capilano River. Feel the bridge sway beneath your feet as you take in breathtaking canyon views. Cross at your own pace - there's no time limit.

Cliffwalk

A series of cantilevered and suspended walkways clinging to the granite cliff face high above the canyon. Glass-bottom sections, narrow bridges, and viewing platforms offer heart-pounding perspectives.

Treetops Adventure

Seven suspension bridges attached to old-growth Douglas firs take you through the mid-story of the rainforest. Walk 30 metres above the forest floor among 800-year-old trees.

Story Centre

Learn the fascinating history of the bridge from 1889 to present day. Historical photos, artifacts, and multimedia displays tell stories of pioneers, First Nations peoples, and the bridge's evolution.

Cliffwalk Experience

Added in 2011, Cliffwalk is the park's newest major attraction - a 213-metre journey along a series of narrow cantilevered walkways anchored to the granite cliff face. The engineering is remarkable: 16 anchor points support walkways that jut out over the canyon with no visible supports below.

The experience includes glass-bottom viewing platforms where you can look straight down to the river below, suspended bridges between cliff sections, and viewing platforms with stunning perspectives of the canyon and rainforest. For many visitors, Cliffwalk is actually more thrilling than the main suspension bridge.

Cliffwalk Tips

Cliffwalk is one-way and can get congested during peak hours. Visit early morning or late afternoon for a less crowded experience. The glass panels are safe to walk on but can be slippery when wet - wear appropriate footwear.

Treetops Adventure

Experience the rainforest from a bird's eye view on Treetops Adventure. Seven suspension bridges span between platforms attached to 250-year-old Douglas fir trees, taking you 30 metres above the forest floor for a total journey of 650 feet.

This eco-friendly attraction was built with special tree collar technology that allows the attachment systems to move as the trees grow and sway. No bolts penetrate the trees, and arborists monitor tree health continuously.

Educational stations along the way explain the layers of the rainforest ecosystem, from the forest floor to the canopy. The perspectives of the massive tree trunks and distant canyon are unlike anything at ground level.

Nature Trails

Beyond the signature attractions, the park offers several ground-level trails through the ancient rainforest. These self-guided walks are included with admission and offer a peaceful contrast to the height-based experiences.

Trail Highlights

  • Rainforest Boardwalk: Accessible boardwalk through old-growth forest with interpretive signs
  • Living Forest Exhibit: Learn about the rainforest ecosystem and the plants and animals that call it home
  • Raptors Ridge: Wildlife exhibit featuring rescued birds of prey including owls and hawks
  • Kia'palano Totem Park: First Nations totem poles and cultural exhibits on the far side of the bridge

Canyon Lights - Winter Event

From late November through January, the park transforms into a winter wonderland during Canyon Lights. Hundreds of thousands of lights illuminate the bridge, canyon, and forest, creating a magical atmosphere.

The event includes the world's largest collection of giant light fixtures, the bridge wrapped in lights, and the Treetops Adventure sparkling above the illuminated forest floor. Holiday entertainment, treats, and special effects add to the festive atmosphere.

Book Canyon Lights Early

Canyon Lights is extremely popular - evenings sell out quickly, especially around Christmas and New Year's. Book tickets online at least 2-3 weeks in advance for weekend dates. Weeknight visits are typically less crowded.

Getting There

Capilano Suspension Bridge is located in North Vancouver, about 15 minutes from downtown Vancouver. The park offers multiple transportation options to suit different preferences.

Free Shuttle Bus

A free seasonal shuttle bus runs from downtown Vancouver (Canada Place) with stops at major hotels. Operates year-round, departing every 15-30 minutes. Check the park's website for current schedule and pickup locations. Reservations recommended during peak season.

By Car

From downtown Vancouver, take the Lions Gate Bridge to North Vancouver. Turn right onto Marine Drive, then left onto Capilano Road. The park is about 5 minutes from the bridge. Free parking is available on-site with several lots.

By Transit

Take the SeaBus from Waterfront Station to Lonsdale Quay in North Vancouver. Transfer to bus #236 (Grouse Mountain) which stops directly at the park entrance. Total journey time is about 45 minutes from downtown.

By Rideshare

Uber and Lyft operate in Vancouver and can drop you at the park entrance. The ride from downtown takes 15-25 minutes depending on traffic. Expect to pay $25-40 one-way.

Insider Tips

  • Beat the Crowds: Arrive at opening time (9 AM) or after 4 PM for smaller crowds and better photos
  • Book Online: Reserve tickets in advance, especially during summer and Canyon Lights season
  • Dress in Layers: The forest is cool even in summer, and rain is always possible in Vancouver
  • Wear Good Shoes: Surfaces can be slippery; hiking shoes or sneakers with grip are ideal
  • Don't Fear the Sway: The bridge is designed to sway - it's completely normal and safe
  • Cross Multiple Times: Your admission allows unlimited crossings; experience it at different times of day
  • Combine with Grouse: Grouse Mountain is just 5 minutes away - combine for a full day adventure
  • Annual Pass Value: If you're a BC resident, the annual pass pays for itself in two visits
