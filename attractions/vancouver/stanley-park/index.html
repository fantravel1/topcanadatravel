About Stanley Park

Stanley Park is the crown jewel of Vancouver and one of North America's most beloved urban parks. Spanning 1,001 acres on a peninsula nearly surrounded by the waters of English Bay and Vancouver Harbour, this natural oasis offers an extraordinary escape just minutes from downtown's glass towers.

Named after Lord Stanley, the same Governor General who donated hockey's Stanley Cup, the park was established in 1888 as Vancouver's first park. The land was originally home to the Squamish, Musqueam, and Tsleil-Waututh peoples for thousands of years before European settlement.

Today, Stanley Park welcomes over 8 million visitors annually, making it one of the most visited urban parks in North America. The park combines natural West Coast rainforest with world-class attractions, stunning beaches, and the famous seawall that rings its perimeter.

Old-Growth Forest

Stanley Park contains some of the last remaining old-growth forest on Vancouver's peninsula. Some western red cedars and Douglas firs are estimated to be 500-800 years old, having survived the great fire of 1886 that destroyed most of early Vancouver.

The Stanley Park Seawall

The seawall is Stanley Park's most famous feature - a 9-kilometre paved path that circles the entire park along the waterfront. It's the longest uninterrupted waterfront path in Canada and offers spectacular views of the mountains, ocean, and city skyline.

Construction of the seawall began in 1917 and took over 60 years to complete. Much of the work was done by a single stonemason, James Cunningham, who dedicated 32 years of his life to the project. A plaque near Siwash Rock commemorates his remarkable contribution.

Seawall Highlights

  • Lions Gate Bridge Viewpoint: Stunning views of the iconic suspension bridge connecting Vancouver to the North Shore
  • Siwash Rock: A 32-million-year-old sea stack with deep significance to the Squamish people
  • Prospect Point: The highest point on the seawall with panoramic views and a cafe
  • Third Beach: Popular swimming beach with legendary sunset views
  • Brockton Point Lighthouse: Historic 1914 lighthouse and totem poles

Seawall Direction

The seawall is one-way for cyclists (counter-clockwise) but walkers can go either direction. For the best experience, walk clockwise starting from Coal Harbour - you'll have the ocean views on your right and finish with spectacular sunset angles at Third Beach.

Vancouver Aquarium

Located in the heart of Stanley Park, the Vancouver Aquarium is Canada's largest aquarium and a leader in marine research and conservation. Home to over 65,000 animals representing 700+ species, it offers intimate encounters with Pacific marine life.

Aquarium Highlights

  • Pacific Canada Pavilion: Sea otters, harbour seals, and Steller sea lions in outdoor exhibits
  • Amazon Gallery: Tropical fish, caimans, and sloths in a rainforest setting
  • Discover Rays Touch Pool: Hands-on experience with cownose rays
  • Penguin Point: African penguins in a specially designed habitat
  • Jellyfish Gallery: Mesmerizing displays of ethereal jellyfish species

The aquarium's Marine Mammal Rescue Centre has rehabilitated over 200 marine mammals each year, releasing them back into the wild. Visitors can learn about these conservation efforts throughout the facility.

Totem Poles at Brockton Point

The totem poles at Brockton Point are among the most visited attractions in British Columbia. This collection of nine totem poles showcases the incredible artistry of Pacific Northwest First Nations peoples including the Squamish, Haida, and Kwakwaka'wakw nations.

The first totem poles were brought to the park in the 1920s and 1930s. Some have been replaced over time to preserve the collection, while others are original pieces over a century old. Each pole tells stories of family histories, legends, and cultural significance.

Interpretive signs explain the meaning and origin of each pole. For deeper understanding, consider joining a guided First Nations cultural tour available through various operators in Vancouver.

Beaches

Third Beach

The most scenic beach in Stanley Park, famous for spectacular sunsets over the Pacific. Less developed than Second Beach with a more natural atmosphere. Popular for swimming, picnics, and evening bonfires (permit required).

Second Beach

Family-friendly beach with a heated outdoor swimming pool ($6.55 adult), playground, and concession stand. The pool is open mid-May to September and offers ocean views while swimming in fresh water.

English Bay Beach

Just outside Stanley Park at its southern entrance, this is Vancouver's most popular beach. Lively atmosphere with restaurants, cafes, and people-watching. Site of the annual Celebration of Light fireworks.

Sunset Beach

A quieter alternative stretching west from English Bay. Great for swimming with gentler waves and stunning views of the North Shore mountains. Connected to Stanley Park via the seawall extension.

Lost Lagoon

Lost Lagoon is a picturesque freshwater lake at the southern entrance to Stanley Park. Once part of Coal Harbour, it was separated from the ocean when the Stanley Park Causeway was built in 1916. The name comes from poet Pauline Johnson, who wrote about the lagoon "lost" at low tide.

Today, Lost Lagoon is a nature sanctuary home to herons, swans, geese, and countless other birds. The 1.7-kilometre path around the lagoon is perfect for a peaceful stroll, especially at dawn when wildlife is most active. The Stanley Park Nature House on the lagoon's shore offers free educational programs about the park's ecology.

Trails and Forest Walks

Beyond the seawall, Stanley Park offers over 27 kilometres of forested trails ranging from easy strolls to more challenging hikes. These paths wind through old-growth forest, past hidden lakes, and through quiet groves far from the crowds.

Popular Interior Trails

  • Beaver Lake Trail: Easy 1km loop around a lily-covered lake, excellent for birdwatching
  • Cathedral Trail: Moderate 1.5km walk through towering old-growth trees
  • Rawlings Trail: Quiet 2km path connecting the interior trails
  • Bridle Path: Wide, easy 4km path through the park's centre, popular with joggers

Trail Safety

Coyotes live in Stanley Park. While attacks are rare, keep small children close, don't feed wildlife, and if approached by a coyote, make yourself large and make noise. Report aggressive wildlife to the Park Board.

Getting There

Stanley Park is located at the northwestern tip of downtown Vancouver, easily accessible by multiple transportation options. The park has limited parking that fills quickly on sunny days, so alternatives are recommended.

By Transit

Take bus #19 (Stanley Park) which loops through the park with stops at major attractions including the aquarium, Prospect Point, and Third Beach. The #5 bus stops at English Bay, a short walk from the park entrance.

By Bike or Foot

The seawall connects Stanley Park to downtown Vancouver. Walk or cycle along the Coal Harbour seawall from Canada Place (about 20 minutes). Numerous bike rental shops are located on Denman Street near the park entrance.

By Car

Enter via Georgia Street which becomes Stanley Park Drive. Parking lots are located throughout the park (pay parking $3.60-$7.50/hour). The main lot near the Vancouver Aquarium fills by 10 AM on summer weekends. Consider parking downtown and walking in.

Free Shuttle

A free shuttle bus runs within the park during peak summer season, connecting major attractions. Check the Stanley Park website for current schedules.

Insider Tips

  • Avoid Crowds: Visit early morning (before 9 AM) or early evening for the most peaceful experience
  • Rent E-Bikes: The seawall has some hills - e-bikes make the 9km loop effortless and fun
  • Bring Layers: Vancouver weather changes quickly; the seawall can be breezy even on sunny days
  • Pack a Picnic: Prospect Point and Third Beach have excellent picnic spots with stunning views
  • Visit Off-Season: Fall and winter offer dramatic skies, fewer crowds, and rainforest at its most atmospheric
  • Combine with Aquarium: Rainy day? The aquarium provides 2-3 hours of indoor entertainment
  • Sunset at Third Beach: Arrive 30 minutes before sunset and claim a spot on the logs

Frequently Asked Questions

The full Stanley Park seawall loop is 9 kilometres (5.6 miles). Walking at a leisurely pace takes about 2-3 hours, while cycling takes approximately 1-1.5 hours. Most visitors stop frequently for photos and viewpoints, so plan for 3-4 hours for a complete experience.

Yes, Stanley Park itself is completely free to enter and explore. There are fees for the Vancouver Aquarium ($42 adult admission), parking ($3.60-$7.50/hour depending on season), and bike rentals ($8-15/hour). The seawall, trails, beaches, and totem poles are all free to enjoy.

Third Beach is the most popular for swimming and sunsets with a natural, relaxed vibe. Second Beach has a heated outdoor pool and playground, perfect for families. English Bay Beach, just outside the park, offers a lively urban beach atmosphere with nearby restaurants.

Yes, dogs are welcome in Stanley Park but must be on leash in most areas. There are designated off-leash areas including sections of Ceperley Meadow and the area near the rose garden. Dogs are not allowed on beaches from June 1 to September 15. Always clean up after your pet.

Stanley Park is generally safe, but like any large urban park, it's best to stick to well-lit, populated areas after dark. The seawall is popular with evening joggers. Interior trails should be avoided at night. Be aware of coyote activity, especially around dawn and dusk.
