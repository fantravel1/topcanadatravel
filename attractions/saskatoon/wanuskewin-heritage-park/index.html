About Wanuskewin Heritage Park

Wanuskewin Heritage Park, whose name means "seeking peace of mind" in Cree, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located just 15 minutes north of Saskatoon. With over 6,400 years of continuous Indigenous history, it stands as one of the longest-running archaeological sites on the Northern Plains, predating the Egyptian pyramids.

The park encompasses a stunning valley along Opimihaw Creek, where Northern Plains peoples gathered for millennia to hunt bison, hold ceremonies, and share knowledge. Today, Wanuskewin serves as a living cultural centre where visitors can connect with Indigenous traditions, walk ancient trails, and witness the return of Plains bison to their ancestral lands.

Archaeological excavations have uncovered 19 pre-contact sites within the park, including a medicine wheel, bison jumps, tipi rings, and habitation sites. These discoveries paint a vivid picture of life on the Northern Plains stretching back thousands of years.

UNESCO World Heritage Designation

Wanuskewin was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in recognition of its outstanding universal value. The site represents one of the most significant concentrations of pre-contact archaeological sites on the Northern Plains, with evidence of continuous human activity spanning over 6,400 years.

The Bison Return

In a landmark conservation and cultural restoration initiative, Plains bison have been returned to Wanuskewin's ancestral lands. For thousands of years, bison were central to the lives of Northern Plains peoples, providing food, shelter, tools, and spiritual significance. Their return to Wanuskewin represents a powerful reconnection with the land's heritage.

Visitors can observe the bison herd from designated viewing areas along the park's trail system. The sight of these majestic animals grazing on the same grasslands where their ancestors roamed for millennia is a profoundly moving experience.

Bison Viewing Tips

Early morning and late afternoon offer the best bison viewing opportunities. Bring binoculars for a closer look. Stay on designated trails and maintain a safe distance at all times. The bison are wild animals and should be respected from afar.

Experiences & Programs

Interpretive Programs

Guided tours led by Indigenous interpreters share stories of the land, its peoples, and their traditions. Learn about archaeology, plant medicine, and the significance of the sacred sites.

Trail Walks

Explore the valley on well-maintained trails that wind past archaeological sites, through native prairie grasslands, and along the creek. Interpretive signage explains the history at each stop.

Traditional Dance & Storytelling

Experience traditional dance performances and storytelling sessions that bring the culture and history of Northern Plains peoples to life. Seasonal programs vary throughout the year.

Indigenous Cuisine

The on-site restaurant serves authentic Indigenous-inspired cuisine featuring bison, wild rice, bannock, and seasonal ingredients gathered from the surrounding prairie landscape.

Trails & Nature

Wanuskewin's trail system takes visitors through a beautiful valley landscape of native prairie grasslands, creek beds, and rolling hills. The trails vary in difficulty, making the park accessible to visitors of all fitness levels.

Trail Highlights

  • Valley Trail: An easy loop through the heart of the valley, passing archaeological sites and bison viewing areas
  • Medicine Wheel Trail: Moderate hike to an ancient medicine wheel site with interpretive displays
  • Bison Lookout Trail: Short walk to an elevated viewpoint overlooking the bison grazing area
  • Creek Trail: Gentle path along Opimihaw Creek, excellent for birdwatching and wildflower viewing in summer

What to Bring

Wear sturdy walking shoes and bring water, sunscreen, and insect repellent in summer. The trails are exposed to the prairie elements, so dress in layers. In winter, the park offers snowshoeing and cross-country skiing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Wanuskewin is approximately 15 minutes north of downtown Saskatoon by car. Take Highway 11 north and follow the signs. There is free parking on-site. Public transit options are limited, so driving or a taxi/rideshare is recommended.

The interpretive centre and some portions of the trail system are wheelchair accessible. However, some trails involve uneven terrain and slopes. Contact the park in advance to discuss accessibility needs and they can recommend the best routes.
