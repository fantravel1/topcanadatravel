About Ice Road Driving

Every winter, when temperatures plunge well below -20¬∞C and the ice on Great Slave Lake grows thick enough to support heavy vehicles, the Northwest Territories government opens seasonal ice roads across the frozen surface. These remarkable highways of ice connect Yellowknife to communities on the other side of the lake and provide a lifeline for remote settlements that are otherwise accessible only by air or summer barge.

For visitors, driving on these ice roads is a bucket-list Arctic experience. The sensation of driving across a vast frozen lake -- with nothing but ice beneath you and endless white horizon in every direction -- is surreal and unforgettable. The main crossing near Yellowknife, the Dettah Ice Road, connects the city to the community of Dettah (about 6 km across the frozen bay), cutting a drive that would otherwise take 27 km by land down to a straight shot across the ice.

Ice roads in the NWT gained international fame through the History Channel's "Ice Road Truckers" TV series, which documented the dangerous work of trucking supplies on longer ice roads to diamond mines further north. While the tourist-accessible ice roads near Yellowknife are far tamer than those extreme routes, the experience of driving on frozen water remains uniquely thrilling.

The Dettah Ice Road

The most accessible ice road for visitors is the Dettah Ice Road, crossing Yellowknife Bay on Great Slave Lake. This government-maintained road connects Yellowknife to the Yellowknives Dene community of Dettah. It's plowed, marked with reflectors, and safe for regular vehicles when officially open.

Driving Experience

Self-Drive (Free)

Drive your own or rental vehicle across the Dettah Ice Road. Free to use when officially open. A surreal 15-minute drive across frozen Yellowknife Bay with stunning views.

Guided Ice Road Tours

Join a guided tour with local commentary about ice road history, construction, and the communities they serve. Tours from $80 per person, often combined with other winter activities.

Photography Stops

Pull over at designated spots to photograph the vast frozen landscape. The flat, white expanse creates stunning minimalist compositions, and on clear days the views stretch for kilometres.

Visit Dettah Community

Cross the ice road to visit the Yellowknives Dene First Nation community of Dettah on the east shore. Learn about Indigenous culture and enjoy views back across the bay to Yellowknife.

Safety & Practical Tips

  • Only Use Official Roads: Never drive on lake ice unless it's an officially opened and maintained ice road. The NWT government monitors ice thickness and posts road status
  • Obey Speed Limits: Speed limits on ice roads are strictly enforced. Driving too fast creates dangerous pressure waves under the ice
  • Keep Windows Open: Crack your windows slightly so you can hear any ice sounds and exit quickly in an unlikely emergency
  • Don't Stop on the Ice: Avoid stopping for extended periods in one spot, as the static weight can stress the ice. Pull over only at designated stopping areas
  • Remove Seatbelts: Some ice road driving guidance recommends unfastening seatbelts for the crossing so you can exit quickly if needed, though official guidance varies
  • Winter Tires: Essential for traction on the ice surface. All-season tires are inadequate
  • Check Road Status: Ice road opening and closing dates vary each year. Check the NWT Department of Infrastructure website for current road conditions

Rental Car Note

Some car rental companies in Yellowknife restrict ice road driving in their rental agreements. Check with your rental company before driving on ice roads. Local operators familiar with the conditions are generally more accommodating than national chains.
