About the Heritage Centre

The Prince of Wales Northern Heritage Centre is the Northwest Territories' premier museum, archives, and cultural institution. Located on the shore of Frame Lake in Yellowknife, this modern facility houses comprehensive collections and exhibits that tell the story of Canada's North -- from ancient geological formations and Ice Age megafauna to the rich cultures of the Dene, Inuvialuit, and Metis peoples, the gold rush era, and the modern North.

Opened in 1979 and named after Prince Charles (then Prince of Wales), the centre serves as both a public museum and the territory's official archives. Its collections include over 100,000 artifacts, photographs, maps, and documents spanning thousands of years of northern history and culture.

For visitors to Yellowknife, the Heritage Centre provides essential context for understanding the land, cultures, and history of the Northwest Territories. It's particularly valuable as a first stop before exploring the region, helping visitors appreciate the deep history and cultural significance of the places they'll visit.

Free Admission -- Always

The Prince of Wales Northern Heritage Centre is funded by the Government of the Northwest Territories and offers free admission to all visitors, year-round. Donations are welcome and support the museum's programming and conservation work.

Exhibits & Collections

Indigenous Cultures Gallery

The centrepiece of the museum, featuring Dene, Inuvialuit, and Metis cultural artifacts, traditional clothing, tools, artwork, and oral histories. Learn about the peoples who have called the North home for millennia.

Aviation Heritage

The North was opened by bush pilots who flew supplies and people into remote communities. This exhibit honours their courage with aircraft displays, personal stories, historic photos, and navigation equipment.

Natural History & Geology

Explore the geological forces that shaped the NWT, from ancient rock formations to diamond-bearing kimberlite pipes. Displays feature Arctic wildlife, fossils, mineral specimens, and the territory's mining history.

Northern History

From the fur trade era through the gold rush to modern resource development, trace the history of European contact and settlement in the Northwest Territories. Includes exhibits on the development of Yellowknife.

Planning Your Visit

Getting There

The Heritage Centre is located at 4750 48th Street, on the shore of Frame Lake. It's a 15-minute walk from downtown Yellowknife or a short drive with free on-site parking. The building is visible from the Frame Lake Trail, making it easy to combine with a lakeside walk.

What to See First

Start with the Indigenous Cultures Gallery for the deepest understanding of the NWT's heritage. The aviation exhibit is a favourite for visitors interested in the pioneering spirit of the North. Allow extra time for the gift shop, which carries authentic Indigenous art, crafts, and books.

Special Programs

The Heritage Centre hosts rotating temporary exhibitions, cultural workshops, film screenings, and educational programs throughout the year. Summer programming is particularly rich, with outdoor activities and Indigenous cultural demonstrations. Check their website for current offerings.

Perfect for Cold or Rainy Days

The Heritage Centre is an ideal activity for days when outdoor conditions aren't cooperating. Whether it's -35¬∞C winter cold, summer rain, or smoky skies from forest fires, the museum offers a comfortable, enriching indoor experience. It's also a great warm-up stop between outdoor winter activities.

The Gift Shop

The Heritage Centre gift shop is one of the best places in Yellowknife to purchase authentic Indigenous art and crafts. Items include:

  • Dene beadwork: Traditional moosehide moccasins, mitts, and decorative items
  • Northern art: Prints, paintings, and sculptures by NWT artists
  • Books: Comprehensive selection on northern history, culture, and nature
  • Jewelry: Canadian diamond jewelry and Indigenous-made pieces
  • Children's items: Educational toys, books, and northern-themed gifts

Frequently Asked Questions

Yes, admission is completely free for all visitors. The museum is funded by the Government of the Northwest Territories as a public institution. Donations are gratefully accepted and support the museum's collections, programming, and conservation efforts.

Most visitors spend 1-2 hours exploring the main exhibits. History and culture enthusiasts may want additional time for the archives, temporary exhibitions, and gift shop. The museum is well-organized and easy to navigate, so even a brief 45-minute visit is worthwhile.

Yes, the Prince of Wales Northern Heritage Centre is fully wheelchair accessible. The building features ramps, elevators, accessible washrooms, and wide corridors throughout all exhibit areas. The parking lot includes designated accessible parking spaces near the entrance.

The museum offers self-guided visits with informative exhibit labels and displays. Group tours can sometimes be arranged in advance by contacting the museum. Staff and volunteers are friendly and knowledgeable -- don't hesitate to ask questions during your visit.

Photography for personal use is generally permitted in most areas of the museum. Some temporary exhibits or specific artifacts may have photography restrictions. Flash photography may be restricted to protect sensitive items. Check with staff if you're unsure about a particular exhibit.
