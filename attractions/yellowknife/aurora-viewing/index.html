About Aurora Viewing in Yellowknife

Yellowknife is the undisputed aurora capital of North America. Sitting directly beneath the auroral oval at 62¬∞ north latitude, this remote capital of the Northwest Territories offers the most reliable Northern Lights viewing on the continent. With over 240 aurora-active nights per year and a remarkable 90% visibility rate over three consecutive nights, Yellowknife draws thousands of aurora chasers from around the world each year.

The city's unique geographic advantages are unmatched: low light pollution, flat terrain providing unobstructed horizons in every direction, and relatively low cloud cover compared to other northern destinations. The aurora here isn't a faint glow on the horizon -- it fills the entire sky with dancing curtains of green, purple, pink, and red light that can appear directly overhead.

Several aurora viewing operators have established dedicated facilities outside the city, known as aurora villages. These purpose-built sites feature heated tipis and cabins where visitors can warm up between viewing sessions, enjoy hot chocolate and bannock, and learn about the science and Indigenous stories behind the Northern Lights.

Why Yellowknife?

Yellowknife sits directly under the auroral oval -- the ring of aurora activity around the magnetic North Pole. Unlike other popular aurora destinations, Yellowknife's location means the Northern Lights appear directly overhead rather than low on the horizon, creating the most spectacular displays possible.

Aurora Tour Experiences

Aurora Village Tours

Visit a dedicated aurora viewing facility with heated tipis, hot drinks, and expert guides. Most popular option with comfortable warming shelters and unobstructed views. From $100+ per person.

Photography Packages

Specialized tours for photographers with tripod-friendly locations, camera settings guidance, and longer viewing times. Some operators offer professional photo portraits under the aurora. From $150+ per person.

Multi-Night Packages

Three-night packages maximize your chances of seeing the aurora. Includes nightly tours, hotel transfers, and sometimes daytime winter activities like dog sledding or snowmobiling.

Lakeside Viewing

Watch the aurora reflected in the waters of Great Slave Lake (summer/fall) or from the frozen lake surface (winter). The double reflection creates stunning photo opportunities.

Best Times for Aurora Viewing

The aurora viewing season in Yellowknife runs from mid-August through mid-April, whenever nights are dark enough. Here's what to expect each month:

Peak Season: December to March

The longest, darkest nights provide the most viewing hours. Temperatures can plunge to -30¬∞C or colder, but heated tipis and proper gear make it comfortable. The aurora is often at its most intense during the equinox months of September and March, when geomagnetic activity peaks.

Shoulder Season: August to November

August and September offer comfortable temperatures (5-15¬∞C) and the chance to see aurora reflected in open water. Nights start getting long enough in mid-August, and autumn provides excellent viewing with less extreme cold.

Spring Viewing: March to April

March is considered one of the best months, combining long dark nights with increasing solar activity. Temperatures begin to moderate, and the Snowking Winter Festival adds daytime entertainment.

Pro Tip: Stay 3 Nights Minimum

With a 90%+ visibility rate over 3 nights, booking at least 3 consecutive nights of aurora tours dramatically increases your chances. Weather can obscure one night, but rarely three in a row. Many operators offer discounted multi-night packages.

Photography Tips

  • Camera Settings: Wide-angle lens (14-24mm), aperture f/2.8 or wider, ISO 1600-6400, exposure 8-25 seconds depending on aurora movement speed
  • Tripod Essential: A sturdy tripod is mandatory. Bring one that won't freeze and stick to your hands -- wrap the legs in foam
  • Extra Batteries: Cold drains batteries rapidly. Keep 3-4 spare batteries warm inside your jacket and rotate them
  • Manual Focus: Set focus to infinity before it gets dark. Use a bright star or distant light to confirm sharpness, then tape the focus ring
  • Intervalometer: Use a remote shutter or built-in intervalometer to avoid camera shake and capture time-lapses
  • Protect Your Gear: When returning indoors, seal your camera in a zip-lock bag to prevent condensation from forming on cold glass

What to Wear for Aurora Viewing

Winter aurora viewing means spending several hours outdoors in temperatures that can reach -30¬∞C to -40¬∞C. Proper layering is essential:

  • Base Layer: Merino wool or synthetic thermal underwear (top and bottom)
  • Mid Layer: Fleece jacket and insulated pants
  • Outer Layer: Expedition-grade parka rated to -40¬∞C and insulated snow pants
  • Feet: Two pairs of wool socks and insulated winter boots (rated to -40¬∞C)
  • Hands: Liner gloves plus expedition mittens. Photographer gloves with flip-back fingertips are ideal
  • Head: Balaclava or warm toque covering ears, plus parka hood
  • Rental Available: Most tour operators rent extreme cold weather gear if you don't have your own
