About the Toronto Islands

The Toronto Islands are a car-free archipelago of 15 interconnected islands in Lake Ontario, just a 13-minute ferry ride from downtown Toronto. This 820-acre urban park offers a peaceful escape from the city with beaches, walking and cycling paths, gardens, and the most spectacular views of Toronto's skyline anywhere.

Originally a peninsula connected to the mainland, the islands were formed when a series of storms in the 1850s cut through the sandbar. Today, they're home to parkland, a small residential community on Ward's Island (one of the few such island communities in North America), and beloved attractions including Centreville Amusement Park.

The islands are Toronto's favourite summer escape. On hot weekends, the ferries can have long lineups as families, cyclists, picnickers, and beach-goers flock to the islands for a taste of cottage country just minutes from the city centre. But even on busy days, you can find quiet spots among the island's many paths and hidden beaches.

The View

The Toronto Islands offer the most iconic view of the Toronto skyline, with the CN Tower rising above the financial district. This view is on countless postcards and is particularly stunning at sunset when the glass towers glow golden and orange.

The Three Islands

While there are technically 15 islands, three have ferry service and distinct characters. All are connected by paths, so you can easily explore multiple islands on a single visit.

Centre Island

The busiest island, home to Centreville Amusement Park, Avenue of the Islands gardens, the Franklin Children's Garden, bike rentals, and the most developed beach facilities. Best for families and first-time visitors.

Ward's Island

A quieter, more residential feel with charming cottage-like homes, the lovely Ward's Island Beach, Island Cafe, and winding garden paths. Popular with locals seeking a peaceful retreat.

Hanlan's Point

The western tip of the islands, featuring Toronto's only official clothing-optional beach. Also has a regular beach area, picnic spots, and is popular with the LGBTQ+ community. Close to Billy Bishop Airport.

Gibraltar Point

Accessible by foot or bike from Centre Island, this area features the historic Gibraltar Point Lighthouse (1808), one of Toronto's oldest structures. Great for quiet walks and birdwatching.

Beaches

The Toronto Islands have several supervised beaches along their southern shore, offering clean sand, calm Lake Ontario waters, and lifeguard services during summer months.

Beach Options

  • Centre Island Beach: The largest and most popular beach with full facilities, concessions, and shallow water perfect for children
  • Ward's Island Beach: A quieter alternative with a local feel, beloved by island residents and those seeking peace
  • Hanlan's Point Beach: Features both a family-friendly section and Canada's largest urban clothing-optional beach
  • Gibraltar Point Beach: Secluded and less developed, ideal for those wanting to escape the crowds

Beach Tips

Water quality is tested daily in summer‚Äîcheck the city's website or signs at the beach before swimming. Bring everything you need as beach facilities are basic. The best swimming conditions are usually in August when the lake has warmed up.

Centreville Amusement Park

Centreville is a charming family amusement park on Centre Island that has delighted Toronto children since 1967. The park features over 30 rides and attractions designed specifically for children aged 2-12, making it one of the best family outings in the city.

Attractions Include

  • Antique Carousel: A beautifully restored 1907 carousel with hand-carved animals
  • Log Flume: A gentle water ride perfect for hot summer days
  • Swan Boats: Pedal boats on the island lagoon with skyline views
  • Miniature Train: A classic train ride around the park
  • Scrambler & Tilt-A-Whirl: Classic midway rides for older children
  • Far Enough Farm: A petting zoo with farm animals

Centreville uses an all-day wristband system (approximately $35-40) or individual ride tickets. The park is open May through September, with extended hours during summer weekends. Check their website for current hours and pricing.

Biking the Islands

The car-free islands are perfect for cycling, with paved paths connecting all areas. Biking is the best way to explore the full extent of the islands in a single visit.

Bike Rental Options

Several rental shops operate on Centre Island near the ferry dock, offering:

  • Single Bikes: Standard adult and children's bikes
  • Tandem Bikes: Two-person bikes for couples or parent-child riding
  • Quadricycles: Four-wheeled pedal vehicles seating 2-4 people
  • Kids' Bikes: Various sizes with training wheels available
  • Electric Bikes: Available at some locations for easier touring

Rates are typically $10-15 per hour for single bikes, with multi-hour discounts available. Bring your own bike on the ferry for free (restrictions may apply on peak summer weekends).

Suggested Bike Route

Start at Centre Island, head west to Hanlan's Point (about 2 km), then return east all the way to Ward's Island (about 3 km). This route covers the full length of the islands and offers varied scenery‚Äîabout 10 km total and 1-2 hours of leisurely riding with stops.

Getting There - Ferry Information

The only public access to the Toronto Islands is by ferry from the Jack Layton Ferry Terminal (formerly Toronto Island Ferry Terminal) at the foot of Bay Street on Queens Quay.

Ferry Destinations

  • Centre Island: Most frequent service, especially in summer (every 15-30 minutes)
  • Ward's Island: Year-round service, less frequent (every 30-60 minutes)
  • Hanlan's Point: Seasonal service, primarily summer (every 30-60 minutes)

Important Ferry Tips

  • Buy Online: Purchase tickets in advance at the City of Toronto website to skip the ticket line
  • Arrive Early: On summer weekends, lineups can exceed an hour‚Äîarrive 30-60 minutes before your target ferry
  • Last Ferry: Note the time of the last return ferry (around 11 PM in summer, earlier in off-season)
  • Roundtrip Included: Your ticket includes return; keep it safe

Beat the Crowds

For the smoothest experience, visit on weekday mornings or take the Ward's Island ferry instead of Centre Island‚Äîit's usually less crowded and Ward's is just as beautiful. You can walk or bike to Centre Island if needed.

What to Bring

The islands have limited services, so come prepared:

  • Picnic Supplies: Bring your own food and drinks‚Äîthere are many beautiful picnic spots but limited dining options
  • Sunscreen & Hats: Little shade on the beaches and paths
  • Cash: Some vendors and bike rentals prefer or require cash
  • Beach Gear: Towels, swimsuits, and beach toys if planning to swim
  • Layers: Lake breezes can make it cooler than downtown
  • Water: Stay hydrated, especially on hot days
  • Camera: The skyline views demand good photos

Seasonal Considerations

Summer (June-August)

Peak season with full services, lifeguards, all ferries running, and Centreville open. Expect crowds on weekends. Best swimming and beach weather.

Spring & Fall

Beautiful for cycling and walking with fewer crowds. Some services reduced. Spring brings blooming gardens; fall offers stunning colours.

Winter

Only Ward's Island ferry operates (for residents). The islands are quiet and peaceful for winter walks, but dress warmly‚Äîlake winds are cold.

Photography Times

Sunset from Centre Island or Ward's Island offers the most spectacular skyline photos. Early morning has beautiful light and no crowds.
