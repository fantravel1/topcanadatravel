About Province House

Province House is the single most important building in the story of Canadian Confederation. In September 1864, 23 delegates from Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Prince Edward Island, and the Province of Canada gathered in its legislative chamber for the Charlottetown Conference ‚Äî the historic meeting that planted the seed for the creation of Canada.

Built between 1843 and 1847 in the Georgian neoclassical style, Province House served as PEI's colonial legislature and continued as the provincial legislature until recent restoration work. The building's elegant sandstone facade and symmetrical design reflect the importance of democratic governance in colonial British North America.

The restored Confederation Chamber is the centrepiece of any visit. Standing in the room where the Fathers of Confederation debated the future of a nation is a profoundly moving experience. Period furnishings, original documents, and interpretive displays transport visitors back to those pivotal September days of 1864.

The 1864 Charlottetown Conference

The conference was originally planned to discuss Maritime union only. Delegates from the Province of Canada (John A. Macdonald, George-Etienne Cartier, and others) asked to attend and proposed a much grander vision ‚Äî a confederation of all British North American colonies. Their arguments were so compelling that the Maritime delegates agreed to pursue this larger dream, leading to Canadian Confederation on July 1, 1867.

What to See Inside

Confederation Chamber

The restored room where the 1864 conference took place, furnished with period pieces and original artifacts from that historic meeting.

Legislative Assembly

PEI's historic legislative assembly chamber, one of the smallest and most intimate parliamentary spaces in Canada.

Guided Tours

Knowledgeable Parks Canada interpreters bring the story of Confederation to life with engaging tours in English and French.

Heritage Exhibits

Displays featuring original documents, photographs, and artifacts from the Confederation era and PEI's political history.

Visiting Tips

  • Restoration Status: Province House has been undergoing major restoration by Parks Canada. A temporary visitor centre nearby offers Confederation exhibits during closure periods ‚Äî check Parks Canada's website before visiting
  • Best Time: Visit in the morning for smaller tour groups and a more intimate experience
  • Combine With: Walk to Confederation Centre of the Arts next door and Victoria Row nearby for a full Confederation heritage experience
  • Photography: Photos are allowed inside ‚Äî the Confederation Chamber makes for a memorable shot
  • Accessibility: The ground floor is wheelchair accessible; upper floors may have limited access in the heritage building

Insider Tip

Time your visit for late June through August when Parks Canada offers extended programming, including costumed interpreters portraying the Fathers of Confederation. It's an immersive way to experience the story of Canada's birth.

Historical Significance

Province House was designated a National Historic Site of Canada in 1966, recognizing its role as the birthplace of Confederation. The building is often called the "Birthplace of Canada" and is one of the most significant political heritage sites in the country.

Beyond the 1864 conference, Province House served as PEI's seat of government for over 170 years. The province's legislature, the smallest in Canada with just 27 members, met here until the building closed for restoration.

Getting There

Province House sits in the heart of downtown Charlottetown on Richmond Street, between Great George Street and Queen Street. It's within easy walking distance of all downtown hotels, the waterfront, and the Confederation Centre of the Arts. No car needed.
