About the National Gallery of Canada

The National Gallery of Canada stands as the country's foremost visual arts institution, housing the most comprehensive collection of Canadian art in the world. Located on Sussex Drive overlooking the Ottawa River and Parliament Hill, the gallery is both an architectural landmark and a treasure trove of artistic masterpieces.

The building itself is a work of art. Designed by Israeli-Canadian architect Moshe Safdie and opened in 1988, the structure features dramatic crystalline towers of pink granite and glass that echo the Gothic spires of the nearby Parliament Buildings and the historic Notre-Dame Cathedral Basilica. The Great Hall, with its soaring ceilings and panoramic views, is one of Ottawa's most stunning interior spaces.

The collection spans centuries of art, from medieval European works to cutting-edge contemporary pieces. However, the gallery's greatest strength lies in its Canadian holdings, including the world's finest collection of works by the Group of Seven, Emily Carr, and an ever-expanding collection of Indigenous art that tells the story of First Nations, Inuit, and Metis artistic traditions.

Free Thursday Evenings

Every Thursday from 5 PM to 8 PM, admission to the permanent collection is free for all visitors. It's the perfect opportunity to explore the gallery without the cost, though it can be busier than regular hours.

The Maman Spider Sculpture

Standing guard at the gallery's entrance, the giant bronze spider sculpture Maman (French for "Mommy") has become one of Ottawa's most iconic and photographed landmarks. Created by French-American artist Louise Bourgeois in 1999, this 9.25-metre-tall arachnid is both awe-inspiring and slightly unsettling.

The sculpture represents Bourgeois's mother, who was a weaver, and explores themes of maternal protection, nurturing, and the fragility of life. The spider carries a sac of 26 white marble eggs beneath its body. Maman is one of seven giant spider sculptures Bourgeois created, with others located in London, Tokyo, Seoul, and other major cities.

Photo Tip

For the most dramatic photos of Maman, shoot from below looking up, ideally with the gallery's glass towers or Ottawa's skyline in the background. Early morning and late afternoon light create beautiful shadows through the spider's legs.

Collection Highlights

Canadian Art

The world's most comprehensive collection including the Group of Seven, Emily Carr, Tom Thomson, and contemporary Canadian masters. The galleries trace Canadian art from colonial times to today.

Indigenous Art

A growing collection celebrating First Nations, Inuit, and Metis artistic traditions. Includes historic objects, contemporary installations, and the landmark Canadian and Indigenous Galleries.

European Art

Paintings from the 14th to 20th centuries including works by Rembrandt, Monet, Picasso, and Van Gogh. The Rideau Street Chapel, a reconstructed Gothic chapel, is a hidden gem.

Contemporary Art

Bold works from the 1960s to present day, including installations, video art, and multimedia pieces. The gallery actively acquires cutting-edge contemporary works.

Must-See Works

  • Voice of Fire by Barnett Newman: This controversial 5.4-metre-tall stripe painting sparked national debate when purchased in 1990. Love it or hate it, it's unmissable.
  • The Jack Pine by Tom Thomson: One of Canada's most beloved paintings, depicting a windswept pine tree silhouetted against a sunset sky.
  • Rideau Street Convent Chapel: A complete 19th-century Gothic Revival chapel, saved from demolition and reconstructed inside the gallery.
  • Group of Seven Galleries: Entire rooms dedicated to Canada's most famous art movement, featuring iconic landscapes by Lawren Harris, A.Y. Jackson, and others.
  • The Death of General Wolfe by Benjamin West: This massive 1770 painting depicts the death of British General James Wolfe at the Battle of Quebec.
  • Indigenous Art Galleries: Contemporary and traditional works exploring Indigenous perspectives, histories, and artistic innovations.

Getting There

The National Gallery of Canada is located on Sussex Drive in downtown Ottawa, within walking distance of Parliament Hill, ByWard Market, and the Rideau Canal.

By Transit

Take OC Transpo bus routes to Sussex/St. Patrick or walk from the Rideau LRT station (15-minute walk through ByWard Market). Multiple bus routes stop nearby.

By Car

The gallery has an underground parking garage accessible from St. Patrick Street ($3/half hour, $18 maximum). Street parking on Sussex Drive has metered spots. ByWard Market parking lots are a 10-minute walk.

On Foot

The gallery is a pleasant 15-minute walk from Parliament Hill along Sussex Drive, passing the National War Memorial and ByWard Market. The surrounding area is very walkable.

Visitor Tips

  • Start with Canadian Art: If time is limited, prioritize the Canadian and Indigenous galleries - this is what makes the NGC unique.
  • Free Thursday Strategy: Arrive at 5 PM when doors open for free admission to avoid the biggest crowds.
  • Audio Guide: The free audio guide app enhances the experience with stories behind the artworks.
  • Great Hall Photos: Don't miss the stunning views from the Great Hall windows overlooking Parliament Hill.
  • Combine with ByWard Market: The gallery is a short walk from the market - perfect for lunch before or after your visit.
  • Check for Special Exhibitions: Blockbuster exhibitions may require timed tickets and additional fees.
  • Cafe and Restaurant: The gallery cafe offers light fare with views; reserve ahead for the upscale restaurant.
