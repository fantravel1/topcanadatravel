About the Rideau Canal

The Rideau Canal is a masterpiece of engineering and one of Canada's most cherished landmarks. Stretching 202 kilometres from Ottawa to Kingston, this UNESCO World Heritage Site is the oldest continuously operated canal system in North America and the only one from the great canal-building era that remains operational.

Built between 1826 and 1832 under the direction of Lieutenant Colonel John By of the Royal Engineers, the canal was constructed as a strategic military route following the War of 1812. It provided a secure supply and communication route between Montreal and the British naval base in Kingston, bypassing the vulnerable St. Lawrence River. Remarkably, most of the original structures remain intact and functional today.

In Ottawa, the canal cuts through the heart of the city, creating a unique urban waterway lined with historic buildings, parks, and pathways. In winter, a 7.8-kilometre section transforms into the world's largest naturally frozen skating rink, drawing hundreds of thousands of skaters each season. The Rideau Canal was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2007.

World Record Skating Rink

The Rideau Canal Skateway covers 7.8 km and approximately 165,621 square metres of ice, equivalent to 90 Olympic-sized hockey rinks. It holds the Guinness World Record as the world's largest naturally frozen ice rink.

Winter: The Skateway Experience

Every winter, when temperatures drop and the ice thickens to at least 30 centimetres, the Rideau Canal transforms into the legendary Skateway. This quintessential Canadian experience attracts over a million visits each season, with locals commuting to work on skates and tourists from around the world lacing up for the first time.

The Skateway stretches from the Ottawa Locks at Parliament Hill to Dows Lake Pavilion, passing under historic bridges and past some of Ottawa's most iconic landmarks. Along the way, heated shelters, change huts, and food vendors (including the famous BeaverTails pastry stands) keep skaters warm and fueled.

Skateway Tips

  • Check Conditions: Ice conditions change daily. Check the NCC website or call the Skateway hotline before visiting.
  • Rent Skates: Multiple rental locations along the canal offer skates for about $20-25 CAD. Some hotels also offer complimentary rentals to guests.
  • Dress Warmly: Wind on the canal can be bitter. Wear layers, warm gloves, a hat, and consider a face covering.
  • Try BeaverTails: These fried dough pastries topped with cinnamon sugar or Nutella are a Skateway tradition.
  • Start at Dows Lake: If you're a beginner, the section near Dows Lake is often less crowded and has easy access to amenities.

Winterlude Festival

Visit during Winterlude (usually the first three weekends of February) for ice sculptures, snow slides, concerts, and festive activities throughout Ottawa and Gatineau. The Skateway is the heart of the celebration.

Summer: Boating and Beyond

When the ice melts, the Rideau Canal becomes a busy recreational waterway. Pleasure boats, canoes, kayaks, and paddleboards share the water while cyclists, joggers, and walkers enjoy the scenic pathways that line both banks.

Boating

Navigate the entire 202 km from Ottawa to Kingston through 47 locks. Rent boats at Dows Lake or bring your own. The journey takes 3-5 days one way.

Cycling

The canal pathways are part of Ottawa's extensive cycling network. Rent a bike and ride from Parliament Hill to Dows Lake and beyond.

Walking Tours

Stroll along the canal banks, watch boats pass through the Ottawa Locks, and explore the Bytown Museum in the oldest stone building in Ottawa.

Paddling

Rent kayaks, canoes, or paddleboards at Dows Lake and explore the canal from water level. Evening paddles offer beautiful sunset views.

The Ottawa Locks

The Ottawa Locks, located between Parliament Hill and the Chateau Laurier hotel, are the canal's most famous feature. This flight of eight locks raises or lowers boats 24 metres between the Ottawa River and the canal, using the same hand-operated mechanisms installed in the 1830s.

Watching the locks in operation is free and fascinating. Parks Canada staff operate the locks manually, just as they have for nearly 200 years. It takes about 90 minutes for a boat to pass through all eight locks. The Bytown Museum, located in the oldest stone building in Ottawa, tells the story of the canal's construction.

Getting There

The Rideau Canal runs through central Ottawa, making it accessible from almost anywhere in the city. The most popular access points are the Ottawa Locks near Parliament Hill and Dows Lake.

Ottawa Locks (Parliament Hill)

The locks are steps from Parliament Hill, accessible from Wellington Street or by walking down from the Chateau Laurier hotel. This is the best spot to watch the locks in operation.

Dows Lake

Take OC Transpo bus routes to Dows Lake (routes vary seasonally). The pavilion has parking, skate rentals, restaurants, and boat rentals. Free parking is available in winter for skaters.

Along the Canal

Multiple access points exist along the canal, including at Fifth Avenue, Patterson Creek, and the Pretoria Bridge. The Confederation Line LRT runs parallel to part of the canal.

Visitor Tips

  • Check Ice Status: Before skating, always check the NCC's official Skateway status. Conditions change with weather.
  • Weekday Skating: Visit on weekday mornings for the smallest crowds and best ice conditions.
  • Bring Cash: Some food vendors and rental locations are cash-only.
  • Wear Sunscreen: Reflection off ice and snow can cause sunburn, even in winter.
  • Lock Schedule: Locks operate May through October. The first and last boats of the day depart at set times.
  • Bike Rentals: Summer bike rentals are available at Dows Lake and through Ottawa's bike-share system.
