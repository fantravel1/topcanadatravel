About the Canadian Museum of History

The Canadian Museum of History is the country's most popular cultural institution, welcoming over 1.3 million visitors annually. Located in Gatineau, Quebec, just across the Ottawa River from Parliament Hill, the museum offers a comprehensive journey through Canadian history, from the earliest Indigenous peoples to the present day.

The building itself is an architectural masterpiece designed by Indigenous architect Douglas Cardinal. The curving, organic forms of the structure are meant to evoke the emergence of the North American continent and the sculpting power of wind and water. The museum occupies a stunning waterfront site with sweeping views of Parliament Hill and the Ottawa River.

Originally opened as the Canadian Museum of Civilization in 1989, it was renamed the Canadian Museum of History in 2012 to reflect its renewed focus on Canadian history. The museum houses over 4 million artifacts and the most extensive collection of totem poles in the world.

Free Thursday Evenings

Every Thursday from 5 PM to 8 PM, admission to the permanent exhibits is free. The IMAX theatre and special exhibitions may have additional charges. It's a great opportunity to explore this world-class museum without the admission fee.

Major Exhibits

Grand Hall

A breathtaking space featuring the world's largest indoor collection of totem poles and six Indigenous houses representing cultures from the Pacific Northwest Coast. Floor-to-ceiling windows offer stunning views of Parliament Hill.

Canada Hall

The centerpiece of the museum, tracing 1000 years of Canadian history through immersive environments. Walk through a Viking settlement, New France village, and Western frontier town.

First Peoples Hall

An extensive exhibition exploring the history, diversity, creativity, and endurance of Canada's Indigenous peoples from time immemorial to the present day.

Canadian Children's Museum

An interactive wonderland where children can explore cultures from around the world. Features a colorful passport adventure through country-themed play areas.

The Grand Hall

The Grand Hall is the museum's most iconic space and one of Canada's great architectural achievements. Six Pacific Northwest Coast Indigenous houses line the hall, each representing a different nation - Haida, Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuxalk, and Gitxsan.

Towering totem poles, some over 12 metres tall, stand as testament to the artistic traditions of the Pacific Northwest peoples. The collection includes both historic poles and contemporary works, showcasing the living continuity of Indigenous carving traditions.

The hall's massive windows frame a panoramic view of the Ottawa River and Parliament Hill beyond, creating a powerful visual connection between Canada's Indigenous heritage and its parliamentary democracy.

Photography Tip

The Grand Hall offers spectacular photo opportunities, especially during golden hour when sunlight streams through the windows. Photos of the totem poles with Parliament Hill in the background are particularly striking.

Canada Hall

Canada Hall takes visitors on a journey through 1000 years of Canadian history using life-size environments that recreate key moments and places. The exhibit spans from the earliest Norse visitors to Confederation and beyond.

Highlights Include:

  • Norse Settlement: A recreation of the Viking presence in Newfoundland around 1000 CE
  • New France: A town square from early French Canada with period buildings and artifacts
  • Acadian Village: Life in the Maritimes before the Deportation
  • Ontario Street: A 19th-century streetscape with shops and businesses
  • Western Settlement: The expansion of Canada into the prairies
  • Pacific Gateway: The arrival of immigrants from Asia and the building of the railway

IMAX Theatre

The museum houses a state-of-the-art IMAX theatre with a massive screen measuring 21 metres wide and 6 storeys tall. The theatre shows a rotating selection of nature documentaries, adventure films, and Canadian-themed content.

IMAX films are an additional charge on top of museum admission, with combination tickets available. Shows run throughout the day, with schedules available at the ticket desk and online. The immersive experience is particularly popular with families.

Getting There

The Canadian Museum of History is located in Gatineau, Quebec, directly across the Ottawa River from Parliament Hill. Despite being in a different province, it's easily accessible from downtown Ottawa.

On Foot

Walk across the Alexandra Bridge from downtown Ottawa (about 15 minutes from Parliament Hill). The walk offers beautiful views of both cities and the river. This is the recommended approach on pleasant days.

By Transit

STO buses from downtown Ottawa stop directly at the museum. Bus routes 33, 35, and 39 offer direct service from various points in Ottawa and Gatineau.

By Car

The museum has a large underground parking garage ($4/hour, $16 maximum). Accessible from Laurier Street in Gatineau. GPS: 100 Laurier Street, Gatineau, QC.

By Boat

In summer, water taxis operate between the Ottawa locks and the museum dock, offering a unique arrival experience with views of Parliament Hill from the water.

Visitor Tips

  • Start Early: Arrive when the museum opens at 9:30 AM for smaller crowds, especially in summer.
  • Begin with Grand Hall: Start your visit in the Grand Hall while the morning light illuminates the totem poles.
  • Plan for Children: If visiting with kids, allocate at least 1-2 hours for the Children's Museum alone.
  • IMAX Timing: Check show times in advance and plan your museum visit around your preferred film.
  • Bring ID: Student and senior discounts require valid ID.
  • Comfortable Shoes: The museum is large - you'll do a lot of walking.
  • Cafe Overlooking the River: The museum cafe has great views of Parliament Hill - perfect for lunch.
  • Coat Check: Free coat check is available - useful since the museum is well-heated.
