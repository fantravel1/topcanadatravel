About Whistler Blackcomb

Whistler Blackcomb is not just North America's largest ski resort‚Äîit's consistently ranked as the continent's best. Spanning two massive mountains connected by the world-record-breaking PEAK 2 PEAK Gondola, this legendary resort offers 8,171 acres of skiable terrain that caters to everyone from first-timers to extreme athletes.

The resort's impressive statistics speak for themselves: over 200 marked runs, 16 alpine bowls, 3 glaciers, and an average annual snowfall of over 11 metres (450 inches). The terrain breakdown includes 20% beginner, 55% intermediate, and 25% expert, ensuring that skiers and snowboarders of all abilities can find their perfect challenge.

Whistler Blackcomb gained international recognition as the host venue for alpine skiing events during the 2010 Winter Olympics. The Dave Murray Downhill course on Whistler Mountain remains one of the most challenging and exciting runs you can experience, following in the tracks of Olympic champions.

2010 Olympic Legacy

Whistler Blackcomb hosted all alpine skiing events for the 2010 Vancouver Winter Olympics. You can ski the same Dave Murray Downhill course where Lindsey Vonn won gold and experience the Whistler Sliding Centre where bobsled, luge, and skeleton events took place.

The Two Mountains

Whistler Mountain

The original mountain that started it all, Whistler Mountain rises to 2,182 metres (7,160 feet) at its peak. It's known for its incredible variety, from gentle beginner slopes at the Olympic Station to the challenging steeps of Whistler Bowl and West Bowl. The Harmony and Symphony zones offer intermediate cruisers with stunning views, while experts gravitate toward the Glacier Bowl and the cliffs of Doom & Gloom.

Blackcomb Mountain

Often considered the more challenging of the two mountains, Blackcomb reaches 2,284 metres (7,494 feet) and features legendary terrain like the Horstman Glacier, 7th Heaven, and the Blackcomb Glacier. The mountain is famous for its steep chutes, cliff drops, and consistent snow conditions. The Glacier Creek and Crystal zones provide excellent intermediate terrain, while experts test themselves on Chainsaw Ridge and Couloir Extreme.

Pro Tip: Which Mountain First?

Start your day on Whistler Mountain for the best morning grooming on runs like Burnt Stew Basin and Green Line. Take the PEAK 2 PEAK to Blackcomb after lunch when the sun has softened the snow on the 7th Heaven zone.

Terrain by Skill Level

Beginner (20%)

The Olympic Station area on Whistler features gentle slopes with magic carpets and slow zones. Upper Olympic has longer green runs with beautiful views. Blackcomb's Easy Out provides a scenic route from mid-mountain.

Intermediate (55%)

Explore the Harmony Bowl zone for wide-open cruising, Symphony Amphitheatre for rolling terrain, and Crystal Ridge on Blackcomb for consistent blue runs. The Jersey Cream zone offers some of the best intermediate skiing in North America.

Advanced (25%)

Challenge yourself in Whistler's West Bowl and Peak zone, tackle the legendary Couloir Extreme on Blackcomb, or explore the backcountry-style terrain in Spanky's Ladder area. The 16 alpine bowls offer endless expert options.

Terrain Parks

Multiple terrain parks including the legendary Blackcomb Terrain Park, Nintendo Terrain Park, and the progressive-learning Habitat Park. Features range from small boxes to massive pro-line jumps and superpipes.

Best Time to Visit

Early Season (Nov-Dec)

Limited terrain but lower prices and smaller crowds. Upper mountain opens first with glacier and alpine terrain. Great for keen skiers who want first tracks of the season.

Peak Season (Jan-Feb)

Maximum snowpack and all terrain open. Best powder days and consistent conditions. Higher prices and larger crowds, especially during holidays and weekends.

Spring Skiing (Mar-Apr)

Longer days, warmer temperatures, and excellent corn snow conditions. Great for park riding and apr√®s-ski. Prices begin to drop while conditions remain excellent.

Late Season (May)

Glacier skiing continues into May with limited terrain. Unique experience of skiing in t-shirt weather. Lowest prices and smallest crowds of the season.

Getting There & Around

From Vancouver

Whistler is 120 km (75 miles) north of Vancouver via the scenic Sea-to-Sky Highway (Highway 99). The drive takes approximately 2 hours in good conditions, longer during winter weather. The highway is considered one of the most beautiful drives in the world, with ocean and mountain views throughout.

Transportation Options

  • Drive: Rental cars available at Vancouver Airport (YVR). Winter tires required November-April.
  • Bus: Epic Rides and Whistler Shuttle offer daily service from Vancouver and YVR
  • Limo/Private Transfer: Premium services available for door-to-door convenience
  • Helicopter: 30-minute scenic flight from Vancouver Harbour

Getting Around Whistler

Once in Whistler, you won't need a car. Free local transit connects all major hotels and neighborhoods to the village and lifts. The pedestrian-only Whistler Village is highly walkable, with most accommodation within a 15-minute walk of the gondolas.

Winter Driving Tip

Winter tires or chains are legally required on the Sea-to-Sky Highway from October through April. Check DriveBC.ca for current road conditions. Allow extra time during storms and consider departing Sunday evening to avoid traffic.

Year-Round Activities

While skiing and snowboarding define winter, Whistler Blackcomb transforms into an adventure playground during summer months. The mountains remain open for sightseeing, hiking, and the world-famous Whistler Mountain Bike Park.

  • Mountain Biking: World's best lift-accessed downhill bike park (May-October)
  • Alpine Hiking: Gondola-accessed trails through wildflower meadows
  • PEAK 2 PEAK: Year-round sightseeing with stunning 360-degree views
  • Glacier Skiing: Limited summer ski camps on Horstman Glacier
  • Via Ferrata: Protected climbing routes on Whistler Mountain
  • Zipline Tours: Soar between the mountains on suspension bridges and ziplines
