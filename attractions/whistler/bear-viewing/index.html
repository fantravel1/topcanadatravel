About Whistler's Black Bears

Whistler is home to one of North America's most accessible black bear populations. Approximately 50-60 black bears live in and around the Whistler valley, making this a remarkable destination for wildlife enthusiasts who want to observe these magnificent animals in their natural habitat.

Unlike the more remote bear viewing locations in British Columbia, Whistler's bears have adapted to living near human development. They frequent golf courses, ski slopes, valley trails, and the areas around Lost Lake, often providing viewing opportunities within minutes of the village. This accessibility makes Whistler unique for bear viewing, though it also demands responsible behavior from visitors.

Whistler's black bears range in color from classic black to cinnamon brown. Adults typically weigh between 90-270 kg (200-600 lbs), with males being significantly larger than females. Despite their size, black bears are generally not aggressive toward humans and will avoid encounters when given the opportunity.

Bear Aware Community

Whistler is a certified "Bear Smart" community with extensive programs to reduce human-bear conflicts. This includes bear-proof garbage bins, fruit tree removal programs, and public education. The result is a healthy bear population that coexists relatively peacefully with residents and visitors.

Where to See Bears

Golf Courses

Whistler's golf courses are prime bear habitat. Bears are frequently spotted grazing on the edges of fairways, especially at dawn and dusk. The Whistler Golf Course and Nicklaus North often have sightings. Non-golfers can view from public paths nearby.

Lost Lake Area

The trails around Lost Lake pass through excellent bear habitat. Early morning and evening walks often yield sightings, particularly in berry season. Bears frequent the meadows and forest edges throughout this area.

Valley Trail

The paved Valley Trail network passes through bear territory. Sections near wetlands and berry patches see regular bear activity. Keep alert while walking or cycling‚Äîencounters can happen anywhere along the trail.

Ski Slopes (Summer)

In summer, bears forage on ski slopes where they eat dandelions, clover, and berries. Views from the gondola sometimes reveal bears grazing on the mountain. The alpine zone offers occasional sightings too.

Bear Viewing Tours

Guided bear viewing tours significantly increase your chances of safe, memorable bear encounters. Expert guides know where bears are likely to be, understand bear behavior, and ensure viewing is conducted responsibly.

What Tours Include

  • Expert Guide: Naturalists with deep knowledge of bear behavior and ecology
  • Transportation: Vehicle access to prime viewing locations
  • Equipment: Binoculars and spotting scopes for distant viewing
  • Education: Learn about bear biology, behavior, and conservation
  • Safety: Guides carry bear spray and ensure safe distances
  • Photography Tips: Help getting the best wildlife photos

Tour Options

  • Morning Tours: 2-3 hours starting at dawn, $100-150 CAD
  • Evening Tours: 2-3 hours before sunset, $100-150 CAD
  • Private Tours: Customized experience, $300+ CAD
  • Photography Tours: Focused on getting the best shots, $200+ CAD

Book in Advance

Popular bear viewing tours fill up quickly, especially during peak season (August-September). Book at least a few days in advance, particularly for weekend tours. Operators include Whistler Photo Safaris, Canadian Wilderness Adventures, and local naturalist guides.

Responsible Bear Viewing

How we behave around bears directly impacts their survival. Bears that become habituated to humans or associate people with food often end up being relocated or destroyed. Responsible viewing protects both bears and people.

The Essential Rules

  • Keep Your Distance: Stay at least 100 metres (300 feet) from all bears‚Äîuse binoculars or a telephoto lens instead of approaching
  • Never Feed Bears: This is illegal and extremely dangerous‚Äîa fed bear is often a dead bear
  • Don't Approach: If a bear approaches you, slowly back away while facing the bear
  • Secure Food: Never leave food unattended; use bear-proof containers when available
  • Make Noise: Talk, sing, or clap while hiking to avoid surprising bears
  • Travel in Groups: Bears rarely approach groups of three or more people
  • Keep Dogs Leashed: Off-leash dogs can provoke bear encounters

Never Get Between a Mother and Cubs

Mother bears with cubs are the most dangerous scenario. If you see cubs, assume the mother is nearby and leave the area immediately. Never attempt to photograph cubs or approach them, even if they appear alone.

Bear Safety

While black bear attacks are extremely rare, knowing how to respond to an encounter is important for everyone spending time outdoors in bear country.

If You Encounter a Bear

  • Stay Calm: Don't run‚Äîthis can trigger a chase instinct
  • Make Yourself Known: Speak in a calm, low voice so the bear knows you're human
  • Back Away Slowly: Give the bear space to leave; avoid direct eye contact
  • Don't Climb Trees: Black bears are excellent climbers
  • If Charged: Most charges are bluffs‚Äîstand your ground, then slowly retreat
  • If Attacked: Fight back aggressively with black bears; don't play dead

Bear Spray

Bear spray is highly effective at stopping aggressive bears. Carry it in an accessible holster (not in your backpack), know how to use it before you need it, and check the expiration date. Most outdoor shops in Whistler sell bear spray and can demonstrate proper use.

Report Bear Sightings

Report bear sightings to the Whistler Bear Smart hotline. This helps wildlife managers track bear movements and address potential conflicts. Report any bears displaying unusual behavior, bears near garbage or food sources, or bears that approach people.

Best Time for Bear Viewing

Spring (May-June)

Bears emerge from hibernation hungry and active. Look for them grazing on new grass, dandelions, and skunk cabbage in meadows and avalanche slopes. Cubs born in winter are with mothers and extremely cute.

Summer (July-August)

Berry season begins in late July, making bears more active and visible. They spend hours eating huckleberries, blueberries, and salmon berries. Peak viewing period with longest daylight hours.

Fall (September-October)

Hyperphagia‚Äîbears eat up to 20 hours daily preparing for hibernation. They're focused on food and often less cautious. Late berry crops and spawning salmon (in nearby rivers) provide abundant food sources.

Daily Timing

Dawn and dusk are the most active feeding times. Midday bears often rest in forest shade. For best viewing, be out at first light or in the last two hours before sunset when bears move between feeding areas.

Photography Tips

  • Use a Telephoto Lens: A 200-400mm lens lets you capture detail while maintaining safe distance
  • Shoot in Burst Mode: Bears move quickly; continuous shooting captures the best moments
  • Focus on the Eyes: Sharp eyes make the difference between a good and great wildlife photo
  • Early/Late Light: Golden hour provides the most flattering, dramatic lighting
  • Include Environment: Wide shots showing bears in their habitat tell a complete story
  • Be Patient: Wait for interesting behavior rather than rushing shots
  • Never Bait: Using food to attract bears is illegal and dangerous

Ethical Wildlife Photography

The welfare of the bear always comes before the photograph. If your presence is affecting the bear's behavior‚Äîcausing it to stop feeding, move away, or show stress‚Äîyou're too close. Back off and use a longer lens instead.
