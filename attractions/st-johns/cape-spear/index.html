About Cape Spear

Standing at Cape Spear, you are at the easternmost point of the entire North American continent. At 52 degrees 37 minutes west longitude, this windswept headland juts into the Atlantic Ocean, closer to Ireland than to Canada's west coast. The nearest land to the east is the coast of Ireland, some 3,400 kilometres across open ocean.

Cape Spear is a National Historic Site that combines stunning natural beauty with rich history. The site is home to Newfoundland's oldest surviving lighthouse, built in 1836 and faithfully restored to its 19th-century appearance. A newer lighthouse (1955) still guides ships today. The headland also preserves World War II gun battery emplacements that once defended the approaches to St. John's Harbour.

For many visitors, the most powerful experience is simply standing at the edge of the continent, watching the Atlantic waves crash against ancient rocks, and feeling the raw power of the ocean. In season, humpback whales feed just offshore, icebergs drift past, and the sunrise here is the first to touch North America each morning.

First Sunrise in North America

Cape Spear is the first place in North America to see the sunrise each day. In summer, sunrise can be as early as 5:00 AM. Many visitors make the pre-dawn drive from St. John's to witness this unforgettable moment--watching the sun emerge from the Atlantic at the edge of the continent.

The Historic Lighthouse

The Cape Spear Lighthouse, built in 1836, is the oldest surviving lighthouse in Newfoundland and Labrador. This square wooden structure has been restored to its mid-19th-century appearance, with the lighthouse keeper's living quarters on the ground floor and the light mechanism above.

Inside, Parks Canada exhibits tell the story of the lighthouse keepers who lived and worked at this remote, windswept station for over 150 years. The Cantwell family served as keepers for an remarkable 150 years, from the lighthouse's construction until automation in 1955.

The New Lighthouse

The current operational lighthouse, a concrete tower built in 1955, replaced the original and continues to guide ships approaching St. John's. Its light is visible for over 30 nautical miles.

What to See & Do

Whale Watching (Jun-Aug)

Humpback whales come incredibly close to shore at Cape Spear to feed on capelin. You can often see them breaching, tail-slapping, and feeding from the clifftop viewpoints--no boat required.

Iceberg Spotting (Apr-Jun)

Watch 10,000-year-old icebergs drift past the easternmost point of the continent. Cape Spear's elevated position provides excellent long-range views of Iceberg Alley.

WWII Gun Batteries

Explore the remains of World War II coastal defence batteries, including two 10-inch gun emplacements and underground bunkers that protected St. John's Harbour from German U-boats.

East Coast Trail

Cape Spear connects to the East Coast Trail network. The Cape Spear Path (10 km) follows the dramatic coastline south to Maddox Cove with stunning ocean views and seabird colonies.

Getting There

Cape Spear is located 15 kilometres south of downtown St. John's, about a 20-minute drive via Blackhead Road. There is no public transit to Cape Spear, so you will need a car, taxi, or tour.

By Car

From downtown St. John's, follow Water Street south, which becomes Blackhead Road (Route 11). Follow signs to Cape Spear National Historic Site. The road is paved all the way to the parking area. Parking costs $7.90 per vehicle (Parks Canada fees, mid-May to mid-October).

By Tour

Several St. John's tour operators include Cape Spear on city sightseeing tours. This is a convenient option if you don't have a rental car. Tours typically combine Cape Spear with Signal Hill and other city highlights.

What to Bring

Cape Spear is one of the windiest places in Newfoundland. Even in summer, bring a warm jacket and wind-proof layer. Sturdy shoes are essential for the trails. Also bring binoculars for whale and iceberg spotting, and a camera with a good zoom lens.
