About Petit Champlain

Tucked beneath the towering cliffs of Cap Diamant in Quebec City's Lower Town, Quartier Petit Champlain is a charming maze of narrow cobblestone streets, historic stone buildings, and artisan boutiques. Rue du Petit-Champlain, the neighbourhood's main artery, holds the distinction of being the oldest commercial street in North America, with shops operating here since the early 1600s.

Named after Samuel de Champlain, the founder of Quebec City, this neighbourhood was the commercial heart of New France. Merchants, artisans, and tradespeople lived and worked in the stone buildings that still line these streets today, creating a legacy of craftsmanship that continues in the boutiques and ateliers of the present day.

Today, Petit Champlain is one of Quebec City's most beloved neighbourhoods - a pedestrian-only zone where visitors can wander freely among shops selling Quebec-made crafts, local delicacies, and artisan goods. The area is particularly magical during the Christmas season, when thousands of lights transform it into a winter wonderland.

400+ Years of Commerce

When you walk down Rue du Petit-Champlain, you're following in the footsteps of fur traders, ship captains, and merchants who conducted business here centuries before modern Canada existed. This street has been in continuous commercial use longer than any other in North America.

The Breakneck Stairs

The most famous way to enter Petit Champlain is by descending the Escalier Casse-Cou - the "Breakneck Stairs." This steep wooden staircase plunges down the cliff face, connecting Upper Town's Dufferin Terrace to Lower Town's charming streets below.

The staircase gets its dramatic name from its steepness and the icy conditions that made it treacherous in winter centuries ago. Originally built in 1635, it's one of the oldest staircases in North America. Today it's well-maintained and safe, but the name endures as a reminder of its history.

Breakneck Stairs Facts

  • Built: 1635, rebuilt multiple times since
  • Steps: Approximately 60 steps total
  • Cost: Free to use
  • Best for: Descending (easier than climbing!)
  • Photo op: The view looking up the stairs with overhanging buildings

Avoiding the Climb

If climbing back up doesn't appeal, take the funicular instead. For just $4, you'll glide up the cliff face with views of the river and rooftops. Or simply continue your exploration of Lower Town and find another way back to Upper Town.

The Funicular

For those who prefer to skip the stairs, the Old Quebec Funicular provides a unique and scenic alternative. This inclined railway has connected Upper and Lower Town since 1879, making it one of the oldest funiculars in North America.

The 65-metre ride takes just over a minute and offers wonderful views of Lower Town's rooftops and the river beyond. The glass cabin allows for great photos as you glide up or down the cliff face.

Funicular Details

  • Cost: $4 CAD per ride (one-way)
  • Hours: 7:30 AM - 11 PM (summer), reduced hours in winter
  • Capacity: Up to 20 passengers per cabin
  • Upper Station: Dufferin Terrace, next to Chateau Frontenac
  • Lower Station: Rue du Petit-Champlain, at the base of Breakneck Stairs
  • Accessibility: Wheelchair accessible

Shopping in Petit Champlain

Petit Champlain is the best place in Quebec City to find authentic, locally-made goods. Unlike many tourist districts dominated by souvenir shops selling generic products, this neighbourhood is filled with genuine artisan boutiques where Quebecois craftspeople sell their own creations.

Quebec Fashion & Design

Several boutiques feature Quebec designers creating contemporary clothing, accessories, and jewelry with a distinctly local aesthetic. Look for leather goods, wool items, and winter wear.

Maple Products

Quebec produces 70% of the world's maple syrup. Find pure maple syrup in decorative bottles, maple butter, maple candies, and maple everything at specialty shops.

Indigenous Art

Several shops specialize in authentic Indigenous art, including Inuit soapstone carvings, First Nations prints, and traditional crafts. Always ask about authenticity.

Glass & Pottery

Watch artisans at work in studios selling hand-blown glass ornaments, art glass, and Quebec pottery. Many pieces are created on-site.

Look for the "Quebec Crafts" Seal

Shops displaying the official "Creations Quebecoises" seal guarantee their products are genuinely made in Quebec. This helps ensure your souvenir purchase supports local artisans rather than imports.

Notable Shops

  • Verrerie La Mailloche: Hand-blown glass made on-site since 1975 - watch artisans work
  • Charlevoix Pure Laine: Beautiful wool blankets and clothing from the Charlevoix region
  • Les Delices de l'Erable: Everything maple - the ultimate Quebec edible souvenir
  • Boutique Metiers d'Art: Quebec artisan collective with jewelry, pottery, and art
  • La Cidrerie du Marche: Quebec ciders, ice ciders, and tasting experiences
  • La Petite Cabane a Sucre de Quebec: Maple products and maple taffy demonstrations
  • Pot en Ciel: Colorful Quebec pottery and ceramics

Shopping Tips

Most shops are open 10 AM - 6 PM, with extended hours in summer and during the holiday season. Many accept major credit cards, but some smaller artisan shops prefer cash. Don't be afraid to ask shopkeepers about their products - many are artisans themselves and love sharing their craft stories.

Dining & Cafes

Petit Champlain offers a delightful mix of restaurants, bistros, and cafes where you can fuel up between shopping excursions. The neighbourhood specializes in traditional Quebec cuisine alongside more contemporary fare.

Dining Options

  • Le Cochon Dingue: Beloved local chain serving French bistro fare and Quebec classics
  • Le Lapin Saute: Rabbit-focused fine dining in a charming historic building
  • Cafe-Bistro du Cap: Casual cafe with crepes, salads, and people-watching patios
  • La Cremaillere: Upscale French cuisine in one of the oldest buildings
  • Chocolato: Artisan Quebec chocolates and hot chocolate
  • Paillard: Fresh bakery with sandwiches and pastries (multiple locations)

Must-Try Quebec Treats

Don't leave Petit Champlain without trying maple taffy on snow (tire sur la neige) in winter, a beaver tail pastry, or some authentic Quebec cheese curds. For something warming, try a bowl of pea soup (soupe aux pois) or tourtiere (meat pie).

Seasonal Highlights

Summer (June-August)

Street performers, outdoor patios, and the longest shopping hours. The neighbourhood buzzes with activity well into the evening.

Fall (September-October)

Harvest season brings apple products, fall fashions, and comfortable walking weather. Fewer crowds than summer.

Winter (December-February)

Magical Christmas lights, holiday markets, and cozy shops. Hot chocolate and maple taffy on snow are perfect cold-weather treats.

Spring (March-May)

Sugar shack season means fresh maple products. Watch for maple taffy demonstrations on the street. Patio season begins!

Getting There

Petit Champlain is located in the Lower Town (Basse-Ville) of Old Quebec, at the base of the cliff beneath the Chateau Frontenac.

From Upper Town

Walk down the Breakneck Stairs from Dufferin Terrace (free), or take the funicular ($4). Both options deposit you at the top of Rue du Petit-Champlain.

From Place Royale

It's a 2-minute walk from Place Royale. Head east on Rue Sous-le-Fort and you'll enter Petit Champlain directly.

Parking

No parking in the pedestrian zone. Use the Old Port parking garage or park outside the walls and walk down. The Lower Town is best explored on foot.

Frequently Asked Questions

Petit Champlain can be quite busy, especially during summer afternoons, cruise ship days, and the Christmas season. For a more relaxed experience, visit in the morning before 10 AM, late afternoon after 4 PM, or during the shoulder seasons (May and October). The narrow streets add to the charm but can feel congested at peak times.

The funicular is wheelchair accessible, providing access from Upper Town. Once in Petit Champlain, the main street (Rue du Petit-Champlain) is relatively flat but cobblestoned, which can be challenging. Some side streets are steep. Individual shops vary in accessibility - many historic buildings have steps at entrances.

Plan for 1-2 hours for a casual stroll with some window shopping. Serious shoppers or those wanting to visit multiple artisan studios should allow 2-3 hours. Adding lunch or dinner extends the visit accordingly. The neighbourhood is compact enough to see in an hour if you're short on time.

Prices vary widely. Artisan goods and locally-made crafts are pricier than mass-produced souvenirs, but you're paying for authenticity and quality. Expect to pay $20-50 for small handmade items, $50-200 for quality clothing or larger crafts, and more for fine art or furniture. Budget souvenirs can be found too, but the real value is in genuine Quebec craftsmanship.

The most direct routes are the Breakneck Stairs (free, about 60 steps) or the funicular ($4). Both start at the same spot at the top of Rue du Petit-Champlain and deliver you to Dufferin Terrace, directly in front of the Chateau. The funicular is easier, especially if you're carrying shopping bags!

Most shops accept major credit cards (Visa, Mastercard, American Express), including tap payments. However, some smaller artisan studios may prefer cash, especially for smaller purchases. There are ATMs nearby if needed. Canadian dollars are the currency; US dollars may be accepted at some tourist-oriented shops at unfavorable rates.
