About Old Quebec

Step through the fortified gates of Old Quebec and you'll be transported to another era. Vieux-Quebec is the historic heart of Quebec City and the only fortified city north of Mexico to have preserved its ramparts, gates, and defensive walls. In 1985, UNESCO recognized this extraordinary urban landscape as a World Heritage Site, praising it as "an outstanding example of a fortified colonial town."

Founded by Samuel de Champlain in 1608, Quebec City grew around the natural fortress of Cap Diamant, a massive promontory overlooking the St. Lawrence River. The French, and later the British, built extensive fortifications to protect this strategic location, resulting in the remarkable walls and gates that still encircle the old city today.

Old Quebec is divided into two distinct areas: the Upper Town (Haute-Ville) perched atop the cliff and dominated by the iconic Chateau Frontenac, and the Lower Town (Basse-Ville) nestled along the riverfront where the city was first established. Together, they form one of the most picturesque and historically significant urban landscapes in North America.

Historical Significance

Old Quebec is the cradle of French civilization in North America. Within these walls, the French language, Catholic faith, and distinct Quebecois culture have flourished for over four centuries, surviving British conquest, American invasion attempts, and the pressures of anglophone North America.

The Fortifications

The Fortifications of Quebec represent the most complete military fortification system in North America. The walls stretch 4.6 kilometres around the old city, featuring imposing stone ramparts, four historic gates, and strategically placed bastions that once bristled with cannons.

Walking atop the walls is one of the best free activities in Quebec City. You'll follow in the footsteps of soldiers who patrolled these ramparts for centuries, enjoying spectacular views of the St. Lawrence River, the Laurentian Mountains, and the charming rooftops of the old city below.

The Four Gates

  • Porte Saint-Louis: The most elegant gate, rebuilt in 1878 in a romantic medieval style with towers and battlements
  • Porte Saint-Jean: The busiest gate, leading to the vibrant Saint-Jean-Baptiste neighbourhood
  • Porte Kent: Named after Queen Victoria's father, the Duke of Kent, who lived in Quebec
  • Porte Prescott: The gateway between Upper and Lower Town near the Chateau Frontenac

Wall Walking Tips

Start your wall walk at the Citadelle and head toward Porte Saint-Louis. The views are best in the golden hour before sunset. The walls can be slippery when wet, so wear appropriate footwear.

Upper Town (Haute-Ville)

The Upper Town sits atop Cap Diamant, the dramatic cliff that gives Quebec City its commanding position over the St. Lawrence River. This is where you'll find the city's most famous landmarks, elegant stone buildings, and the gracious Place d'Armes.

Upper Town Highlights

  • Chateau Frontenac: The world's most photographed hotel and Quebec City's defining landmark
  • Dufferin Terrace: A wide boardwalk with stunning river views and street performers
  • Place d'Armes: The historic square surrounded by restaurants and the Chateau
  • Basilica of Notre-Dame de Quebec: The oldest parish church in North America (1647)
  • The Citadelle: An active military base with the famous Changing of the Guard
  • Holy Trinity Anglican Cathedral: The first Anglican cathedral built outside the British Isles

Lower Town (Basse-Ville)

The Lower Town is where Quebec City was born. Samuel de Champlain built his first "Habitation" here in 1608, and for centuries this waterfront area served as the commercial heart of New France. Today, its narrow streets and historic squares are filled with boutiques, restaurants, and museums.

Getting Down

You can reach Lower Town via the famous Breakneck Stairs (Escalier Casse-Cou), the funicular railway ($4), or the winding Cote de la Montagne road. The funicular operates year-round and offers a unique perspective on the cliff face.

Lower Town Highlights

  • Place Royale: The birthplace of French North America with its iconic church
  • Petit Champlain: North America's oldest commercial district with artisan shops
  • Rue du Petit-Champlain: A picturesque pedestrian street lined with Quebec crafts
  • Museum of Civilization: World-class exhibits on Quebec and First Nations cultures
  • Old Port: Revitalized waterfront with markets, festivals, and river cruises

Dufferin Terrace

Stretching along the cliff's edge in front of the Chateau Frontenac, Dufferin Terrace is one of the world's great promenades. This wide wooden boardwalk offers some of the most spectacular views in Canada, looking out over the St. Lawrence River, the distant Laurentian Mountains, and across to Levis on the opposite shore.

Named after Lord Dufferin, a Governor General who championed the preservation of Quebec's fortifications in the 1870s, the terrace is a gathering place for locals and visitors alike. In summer, street performers, musicians, and artists line the walkway. In winter, a thrilling toboggan slide sends riders racing down the icy slopes.

Winter Tobogganing

From December through March, the Dufferin Terrace toboggan slides are a beloved Quebec tradition. Three wooden slides send riders hurtling down at speeds up to 70 km/h. It's pure exhilaration with stunning winter views - a quintessential Quebec experience.

Best Times to Visit

Summer (June-August)

Peak season with outdoor cafes, street performers, and festivals. Long days perfect for exploring. The New France Festival in August brings the 17th century to life.

Winter (December-February)

Magical atmosphere with Christmas markets, ice sculptures, and the famous Winter Carnival. Bundle up for toboggan rides and hot chocolate by the fire.

Fall (September-October)

Fewer crowds and stunning fall colours. Perfect weather for walking the fortifications. Harvest festivals celebrate Quebec cuisine.

Spring (April-May)

Quiet shoulder season with melting snow and emerging patios. Great deals on accommodation. Sugar shack season brings maple treats.

Practical Tips for Visiting

  • Wear comfortable shoes: Cobblestones and steep hills are everywhere - leave the heels at home
  • Learn basic French: While English is widely understood, locals appreciate French greetings
  • Parking: Leave your car outside the walls - street parking is limited and expensive
  • Free walking tours: Several companies offer tip-based tours departing from Place d'Armes
  • Eat like a local: Try poutine, tourtiere (meat pie), and cretons (pork spread) at traditional restaurants
  • Visit early or late: The streets are magical at sunrise before the crowds, and beautifully lit at night
  • Accessibility note: The steep terrain makes wheelchair access challenging; the funicular helps connect the two levels

Money-Saving Tip

Old Quebec is wonderfully walkable and most outdoor attractions are free. Save money by picnicking at Battlefields Park (Plains of Abraham) with treats from local bakeries. The fortifications, Dufferin Terrace, and charming streets cost nothing to enjoy.

Getting There

Old Quebec is located in the heart of Quebec City, easily accessible from all parts of the metropolitan area.

From Quebec City Airport

Jean Lesage International Airport is 16 km from Old Quebec. Taxis cost approximately $35-40 CAD. There's no direct bus, but you can take bus 78 to Les Saules terminal and transfer to a downtown route.

By Car

Highway 40 (Trans-Canada) brings you directly to Quebec City from Montreal (2.5 hours). Park in one of the lots outside the walls - try the Parking Indigo beneath the Hotel de Ville or the D'Youville lot near Porte Saint-Jean.

By Train

VIA Rail's Quebec City station (Gare du Palais) is a beautiful beaux-arts building in the Lower Town, a short walk from Place Royale and the funicular to Upper Town.
