About the Basilica

Rising majestically along the scenic Beaupre Coast northeast of Quebec City, the Basilica of Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre is one of the five national shrines of Canada and the oldest pilgrimage site in North America. For over 350 years, pilgrims have journeyed here seeking the intercession of Saint Anne, the mother of the Virgin Mary and grandmother of Jesus Christ.

The current neo-Romanesque basilica, completed in 1962 after a fire destroyed the previous church in 1922, is the fifth church to stand on this sacred site. Its twin spires rise 91 metres into the Quebec sky, visible for kilometres along the St. Lawrence River. Inside, magnificent mosaics, stained glass windows, and sacred art create an atmosphere of profound reverence.

What draws over half a million visitors each year is not only the stunning architecture but the basilica's reputation for miraculous healings. Near the entrance, columns are covered with crutches, canes, and braces left behind by pilgrims who believe they were healed through Saint Anne's intercession - a powerful and moving testament to faith.

The Miraculous Statue

At the front of the basilica stands a carved wooden statue of Saint Anne from the original 1661 church, known as the "Miraculous Statue." Pilgrims touch or kiss the relic, a piece of Saint Anne's forearm bone preserved in a reliquary, believing in its healing powers. The devotion continues a tradition dating back centuries.

History of the Shrine

The story of Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre begins in 1658, when Breton sailors caught in a terrible storm on the St. Lawrence River prayed to Saint Anne for deliverance. Surviving the ordeal, they built a small wooden chapel on the shore in gratitude - the first church in what would become one of North America's holiest places.

Timeline

  • 1658: First wooden chapel built by grateful sailors
  • 1661: Second chapel constructed; first miracle reported (a crippled man healed)
  • 1676: First stone church built to accommodate growing pilgrimages
  • 1876: Larger basilica constructed as pilgrims numbered in thousands
  • 1887: Pope Leo XIII designated it a minor basilica
  • 1922: Fire destroys the 1876 basilica
  • 1926-1962: Current neo-Romanesque basilica constructed
  • 1960: Pope John XXIII named Saint Anne patron saint of Quebec

The First Miracle

In 1661, Louis Guimont, a local man who had been crippled, placed three stones in the foundation of the second chapel. He was reportedly healed instantly. Word spread throughout New France, and pilgrims began arriving by the thousands. The tradition of healing continues to this day.

Inside the Basilica

The interior of the basilica is a masterpiece of religious art and architecture. Every surface seems to glow with beauty, from the 240 stained glass windows to the intricate mosaic floors depicting scenes from the life of Saint Anne.

Architectural Highlights

The Mosaics

Over 200 million pieces of Venetian mosaic cover the walls and vaulted ceiling, depicting scenes from the lives of Saint Anne, the Virgin Mary, and Jesus. Created by artists from France and Quebec.

Stained Glass Windows

240 stained glass windows flood the interior with colored light. The windows were created by Auguste Labouret and Pierre Chaudiere, among the finest 20th-century glass artists.

The Great Reliquary

Contains a relic of Saint Anne - a portion of her forearm bone. Pilgrims line up to venerate the relic, continuing a devotion practiced here since the 17th century.

Votive Columns

Near the entrance, columns are covered with crutches, canes, and braces left by pilgrims who were healed. A powerful visual testimony to centuries of reported miracles.

The Novena and Feast Day

The most significant time to visit the basilica is during the Novena to Saint Anne, nine days of prayer leading up to her feast day on July 26. During this period, tens of thousands of pilgrims converge on the shrine for special masses, processions, and blessings.

Novena Schedule (July 17-26)

  • Daily Masses: Multiple masses throughout each day in French and English
  • Candlelight Processions: Evening processions around the basilica grounds
  • Blessing of the Sick: Special services for those seeking healing
  • Feast Day (July 26): The culmination with the largest celebrations
  • All-Night Vigils: Prayer services continuing through the night

Planning for the Novena

If you plan to visit during the Novena (July 17-26), book accommodation well in advance - hotels throughout the Beaupre Coast fill up quickly. Expect large crowds, especially on the feast day itself. The atmosphere of collective devotion is deeply moving, whether you're a pilgrim or an observer.

The Grounds & Surroundings

The basilica complex extends beyond the main church to include several other sites worth exploring.

On the Grounds

  • Memorial Chapel: Contains the miraculous statue from the 1661 church
  • Scala Santa: A replica of the Holy Stairs of Rome, climbed on one's knees
  • Way of the Cross: Life-sized bronze statues depicting Christ's path to Calvary
  • Cyclorama of Jerusalem: A 360-degree panoramic painting of Jerusalem at the time of Christ
  • Museum: Houses religious art, historical artifacts, and ex-voto offerings
  • Gift Shop: Religious items, books, and souvenirs

The Cyclorama

Don't miss the Cyclorama of Jerusalem - a remarkable 14-metre high, 110-metre circumference painting created in 1878. Standing in the center, you're surrounded by a stunning panoramic view of Jerusalem as it appeared during Christ's crucifixion. Small admission fee applies.

Visiting as a Tourist

While Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre is primarily a pilgrimage site, tourists of all faiths (or none) are welcome to visit and appreciate the stunning architecture, art, and peaceful atmosphere. The basilica asks visitors to maintain a respectful demeanor appropriate to an active place of worship.

Visitor Etiquette

  • Dress modestly: Shoulders and knees should be covered
  • Silence: Keep voices low and phones on silent
  • Photography: Allowed without flash; avoid photographing worshippers
  • Services: Visitors may attend mass but should remain seated if not participating
  • Candles: Anyone may light a votive candle (small donation)
  • The Relic: Non-pilgrims should allow pilgrims to approach the relic first

Getting There

Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre is located 35 km northeast of Quebec City along the scenic Beaupre Coast (Cote-de-Beaupre), the same route that leads to Mont-Sainte-Anne ski resort.

By Car

Take Highway 440 East from Quebec City, which becomes Highway 138 (Avenue Royale). The basilica is well-signposted. Free parking is available on the grounds. The drive takes approximately 30 minutes.

By Tour

Many Quebec City tour operators offer half-day excursions combining Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre with Montmorency Falls and sometimes Ile d'Orleans. These tours typically last 4-5 hours.

By Bus

Intercar operates bus service along the Beaupre Coast. Check schedules in advance as service may be limited.

Combine Your Visit

Sainte-Anne-de-Beaupre is perfectly combined with a visit to Montmorency Falls (20 minutes away) and/or the ski resort of Mont-Sainte-Anne. The scenic Avenue Royale along the Beaupre Coast is one of Quebec's oldest roads, lined with historic homes and farms.
