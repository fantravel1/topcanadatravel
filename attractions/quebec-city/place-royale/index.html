About Place Royale

Place Royale is the very spot where French civilization in North America began. In 1608, explorer Samuel de Champlain chose this location at the foot of Cap Diamant to build his "Habitation" - a fortified trading post that would become the nucleus of Quebec City and the capital of New France.

For over 150 years, this small square served as the commercial, social, and political heart of the French colony. Wealthy merchants built their homes around the square, ships unloaded precious furs at the nearby wharves, and the colony's most important transactions took place in these stone buildings.

Today, Place Royale is a beautifully preserved window into the past. The square and surrounding streets have been meticulously restored to their 17th and 18th-century appearance, with white stone buildings, steep roofs, and charming shuttered windows evoking the France that Champlain left behind.

The Bust of Louis XIV

The square takes its name from a bust of King Louis XIV (the "Sun King") that was installed here in 1686. The original was destroyed during the British conquest, but a replica now stands in the center of the square, reminding visitors of Quebec's royal French heritage.

Notre-Dame-des-Victoires Church

Dominating Place Royale is Notre-Dame-des-Victoires (Our Lady of Victories), the oldest stone church in North America. Built between 1687 and 1723 on the foundations of Champlain's original Habitation, the church has witnessed over 300 years of Quebec history.

Church Highlights

  • Historic Interior: Original wooden sculptures, paintings, and a stunning altar shaped like a fortified castle
  • The Hanging Ship: A model ship hangs from the ceiling, representing the fleet that brought settlers to New France
  • Two Victories: The church commemorates two French victories over English attackers in 1690 and 1711
  • Rebuilt After Bombardment: Heavily damaged during the 1759 siege, it was faithfully reconstructed
  • Active Parish: The church still holds regular masses and hosts concerts throughout the year

Visiting the Church

Entry is free, but donations are appreciated. The church is open daily but may be closed during services. Check the posted schedule outside. Photography without flash is usually permitted. The interior is particularly beautiful when lit by candles during evening services.

The Fresque des Quebecois

Just steps from Place Royale, you'll find one of the most impressive murals in North America. The Fresque des Quebecois is a massive trompe-l'oeil painting covering 420 square meters of a building wall, creating an astonishing 3D illusion.

Created in 1999 by a team of artists from Quebec and Lyon, France, the mural depicts 400 years of Quebec City's history through the images of notable figures and architectural elements that seem to leap off the wall. You'll spot Samuel de Champlain, Lord Dufferin, hockey players, musicians, and ordinary Quebecois going about their lives across the centuries.

What to Look For

  • Samuel de Champlain: The city's founder, with his maps and instruments
  • Jacques Cartier: The explorer who first claimed Canada for France
  • Changing Seasons: The mural depicts all four Quebec seasons
  • Hidden Details: Look closely for cats, birds, and other surprises
  • Architectural Elements: Windows, balconies, and staircases that fool the eye

Museum of Civilization

A short walk from Place Royale brings you to the Musee de la Civilisation, one of Canada's finest museums. This world-class institution explores Quebec identity, First Nations cultures, and global civilizations through innovative, interactive exhibits.

Museum Highlights

People of Quebec

An immersive permanent exhibition exploring 400 years of Quebec history, culture, and identity through artifacts, multimedia, and personal stories.

First Nations Gallery

Celebrating the rich cultures of Quebec's Indigenous peoples, with artifacts, art, and perspectives from First Nations communities themselves.

Temporary Exhibitions

Rotating exhibitions on topics ranging from global cultures to contemporary issues, always with the museum's signature innovative approach.

Place Royale Centre

The museum operates an interpretation centre in a historic building on Place Royale, with exhibits on the square's archaeological history.

Museum Admission

Adult admission is approximately $22 CAD. Children under 12 are free. The museum is open daily except Mondays in winter. Allow 2-3 hours to explore. The building itself, designed by Moshe Safdie, is architectural marvel worth admiring.

History of Place Royale

The story of Place Royale is the story of New France itself. Here, in 1608, Samuel de Champlain ordered the construction of the Habitation - a three-building complex with a central courtyard that served as trading post, residence, and fort.

Key Historical Events

  • 1608: Champlain builds the Habitation, founding Quebec City
  • 1629-1632: British brothers David and Lewis Kirke briefly capture the colony
  • 1686: The square is named Place Royale when a bust of Louis XIV is erected
  • 1690: The French successfully defend against Admiral Phips' English fleet
  • 1759: British bombardment destroys much of Lower Town during the siege
  • 1860s: The area declines as commerce moves elsewhere
  • 1960s-1980s: Major restoration project returns the square to its historic appearance

Archaeological Treasures

Excavations beneath Place Royale have uncovered remarkable artifacts from 400 years of continuous occupation, including the foundations of Champlain's original Habitation. Many of these treasures are displayed at the Museum of Civilization's Place Royale interpretation centre.

Exploring the Area

Place Royale is the perfect starting point for exploring Quebec City's charming Lower Town. The narrow streets radiating from the square are filled with boutiques, galleries, restaurants, and historic buildings.

Nearby Attractions

  • Rue du Petit-Champlain: North America's oldest commercial street, a 2-minute walk
  • The Funicular: Ride up to the Chateau Frontenac and Upper Town
  • Breakneck Stairs: The dramatic staircase connecting Lower and Upper Town
  • Old Port: Waterfront promenade with seasonal markets and events
  • Rue Saint-Paul: Antique shops and galleries in historic warehouses

Photography Tips

Place Royale is incredibly photogenic. For the best photos of the church and square, visit in the morning when the sun lights the facades. The Fresque des Quebecois is best photographed on overcast days to avoid glare. Come back at night when the buildings are beautifully illuminated.

Getting There

Place Royale is located in the Lower Town (Basse-Ville) of Old Quebec, at the base of the cliff beneath the Chateau Frontenac.

From Upper Town

Take the funicular ($4) from Dufferin Terrace, or walk down the Breakneck Stairs (Escalier Casse-Cou) or Cote de la Montagne road. The descent takes 5-10 minutes on foot.

From the Old Port

Walk along the waterfront promenade and turn inland at Rue Saint-Pierre. Place Royale is a 5-minute walk from the cruise terminal.

Parking

The nearest parking is at the Old Port garage on Quai Saint-Andre, about a 5-minute walk from Place Royale.

Frequently Asked Questions

Place Royale translates to "Royal Square" in English. The name dates to 1686 when a bust of King Louis XIV of France was placed in the center of the square, designating it as royal ground. The name honors Quebec's French royal heritage.

The original Habitation no longer stands, but Notre-Dame-des-Victoires church was built directly on its foundations. Archaeological excavations have revealed the outlines of Champlain's buildings beneath the current structures. The Museum of Civilization's interpretation centre displays artifacts found during these digs.

Plan for 30-60 minutes to explore Place Royale itself, including the church and the Fresque des Quebecois mural. If you want to visit the Museum of Civilization, add 2-3 hours. Combined with exploring the surrounding Lower Town streets, you could easily spend half a day in this area.

Yes, Place Royale itself is wheelchair accessible with a flat, paved surface. The funicular connecting Lower and Upper Town is also wheelchair accessible. However, some surrounding streets have cobblestones that can be challenging. The Museum of Civilization is fully accessible.

Yes, several restaurants and cafes surround Place Royale and line the nearby streets. You'll find everything from casual cafes to upscale French bistros. For the most authentic experience, try traditional Quebecois dishes like tourtiere (meat pie) or maple-glazed salmon.

A major restoration project took place from the 1960s to the 1980s, led by the Quebec government. Many buildings had deteriorated or been altered over the centuries, and the project aimed to return the square to its 17th and 18th-century appearance. The restoration was controversial at the time but is now celebrated as a preservation success.
