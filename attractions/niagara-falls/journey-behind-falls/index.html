About Journey Behind the Falls

Journey Behind the Falls offers one of the most unique perspectives of Niagara Falls - standing inside the rock face, behind the thundering curtain of Horseshoe Falls. This attraction takes visitors 38 metres (125 feet) down through bedrock via elevators, then through 130-year-old tunnels that lead to observation portals directly behind the falling water.

The tunnels were first carved in 1889, making this one of Niagara's oldest attractions. Over the decades, they have been extended and improved, but the fundamental experience remains the same: feeling the immense power of 2,800 cubic metres of water crashing down every second, mere metres from where you stand.

The journey includes two tunnel portals that open behind the falls, plus an outdoor observation deck at the base of the cataract. The combination of the enclosed tunnel atmosphere and the raw, open-air experience of the observation deck creates a truly memorable visit.

Feel the Power

Standing behind Horseshoe Falls, you'll feel the rock walls vibrate from the force of the water. The roar is so intense that conversation is nearly impossible. This visceral connection to the falls' power is unlike any other experience at Niagara.

What to Expect

Your journey begins at the Table Rock Welcome Centre, where you'll receive your recyclable poncho and enter the elevators. The descent through solid bedrock takes about 30 seconds, dropping you 38 metres below the surface to tunnel level.

From the elevator lobby, you'll walk through rock-hewn tunnels to two observation portals. These openings in the rock face are positioned behind the sheet of falling water, giving you an inside-out view of the falls. Through the translucent curtain, you can see the bright light of day and the shapes of the gorge beyond.

The tunnels then lead to the outdoor Lower Observation Deck, positioned at the very base of the falls. Here, without any glass or barriers between you and the water, you'll stand mere metres from where millions of litres crash into the basin every minute. The mist is intense, and the sound is overwhelming - prepare for a sensory experience like no other.

Timing Tip

Visit early morning or late afternoon to avoid peak crowds. The experience is more intimate with fewer people in the tunnels. Winter visits are spectacular - ice formations build up on the tunnel walls and the frozen mist creates an ethereal atmosphere.

The Experience Areas

Cataract Portal

The first tunnel opening positions you behind the main body of Horseshoe Falls. Watch the green-blue water rushing past in an endless curtain. The sheer volume is mesmerizing.

Great Falls Portal

The second portal offers a slightly different angle on the falls. Here the curtain of water is thinner, allowing more light through and creating interesting visual effects.

Lower Observation Deck

Step outside onto the deck at the base of the falls. Feel the mist on your face and hear the thunderous roar without any barriers. The most immersive part of the experience.

Upper Observation Deck

Located at Table Rock level, this deck offers stunning views of the brink of the falls and is included with admission. Perfect for photos and contemplating what lies below.

Best Times to Visit

By Season

  • Summer (June-August): Longest hours and warmest conditions. Most crowded, but the cool mist is refreshing on hot days. Evening visits avoid the worst queues.
  • Spring (April-May): Water levels are often highest due to snowmelt. Fewer crowds than summer. Can be chilly in the tunnels.
  • Fall (September-November): Beautiful fall colours frame the experience. Moderate crowds. Cooling temperatures make the mist feel colder.
  • Winter (December-March): A magical experience with ice formations throughout the tunnels. The falls partially freeze, creating unique views. Dress very warmly.

By Time of Day

  • Opening Time (9-10 AM): Smallest crowds, efficient experience, but you'll miss the late-day light effects
  • Midday: Peak crowds and longest waits, especially during summer weekends
  • Late Afternoon: Crowds thin out, beautiful light conditions
  • Evening (Summer): Extended hours mean you can experience the transition to illumination. The falls are lit up at night and the view from below is spectacular.

Winter Wonderland

Don't skip Journey Behind the Falls in winter! The ice buildup on tunnel walls creates a frozen wonderland. The contrast between the thundering liquid water and the frozen mist formations is otherworldly. Bundle up - temperatures underground are cold but stable.

What to Wear and Bring

  • Footwear: Wear shoes with good grip that can get wet. The tunnels and observation deck can be slippery. No heels or sandals recommended.
  • Layers: The tunnels maintain a cool temperature year-round. Bring a light jacket even in summer. In winter, dress warmly as the cold and mist combine.
  • Poncho: Provided free with admission. It covers your torso but not your legs - expect wet feet and potentially wet hair.
  • Waterproof Phone Case: Essential for photos on the observation deck where mist is heavy.
  • Hair Tie: If you have long hair, tie it back - the wind and mist will tangle loose hair.
  • Minimal Valuables: Leave unnecessary items in your car or hotel. Lockers are not available at the attraction.

Getting There

Journey Behind the Falls is located inside the Table Rock Welcome Centre, the main visitor facility at the brink of Horseshoe Falls on the Canadian side.

By WEGO Bus

Both WEGO Green Line and Red Line stop at Table Rock. This is the most convenient option if you're staying in the tourist district or came by public transit.

By Car

Table Rock has its own parking lot adjacent to the welcome centre. Rates are $30+ CAD per day during peak season. The lot fills early on busy days - arrive by 9 AM in summer.

On Foot

Table Rock is a 15-minute walk from Clifton Hill along the Niagara Parkway. The scenic path follows the gorge with beautiful views. From the Skylon Tower area, it's about a 10-minute walk.

Accessibility

The attraction is wheelchair accessible. Elevators accommodate wheelchairs, and the tunnels are level and wide. However, the Lower Observation Deck may be difficult in a wheelchair due to the wet, slippery conditions and intense mist.
