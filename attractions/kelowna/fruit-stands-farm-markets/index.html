About Kelowna's Farm Scene

Kelowna sits in the heart of the Okanagan Valley, often called Canada's fruit basket. The region's hot summers, mild winters, and fertile benchland soils create perfect conditions for growing tree fruits, berries, and vegetables. Roadside fruit stands are an iconic part of the Okanagan experience -- you'll find them on virtually every highway and country road, overflowing with freshly picked produce.

The farm-to-table movement isn't a trend in Kelowna; it's a way of life. Local restaurants proudly source from nearby farms, and the Kelowna Farmers' and Crafters' Market is one of the largest and best in Western Canada. Whether you're picking your own cherries straight from the tree, sampling artisan cheeses at a farm gate, or enjoying a multicourse meal crafted entirely from local ingredients, Kelowna delivers an authentic agricultural experience.

The Okanagan produces 95% of BC's tree fruits and a significant portion of Canada's cherries, peaches, apricots, plums, pears, and apples. The warm climate also supports nectarines, grapes, watermelons, tomatoes, peppers, and a wide variety of vegetables.

Cherry Capital of Canada

The Okanagan produces the majority of Canada's sweet cherries, and Kelowna is at the centre of it all. During peak season (July), roadside stands overflow with Rainier, Bing, Lapin, Sweetheart, and Staccato varieties. BC cherries are exported worldwide, but nothing beats eating them fresh from a stand minutes after picking.

Kelowna Farmers' Market

The Kelowna Farmers' and Crafters' Market is one of BC's premier outdoor markets, operating since 2000. The Saturday market at the Dilworth Drive location features over 150 vendors selling fresh produce, baked goods, artisan crafts, preserves, honey, flowers, and prepared foods.

Market Details

  • Saturday Market: 8 AM - 1 PM, April through late October (Springfield Road & Dilworth Drive)
  • Wednesday Market: 8 AM - 1 PM, June through October (downtown location)
  • Vendors: 150+ on Saturdays, including farmers, bakers, artisans, and food vendors
  • Highlights: Farm-fresh produce, artisan bread, local honey, preserves, flowers, handmade crafts, and hot food vendors
  • Tips: Arrive early for the best selection. Bring cash (some vendors don't take cards) and reusable bags

Market Tips

The Saturday market is significantly larger than Wednesday. Arrive by 8:30 AM for the best selection of seasonal produce. Parking fills quickly -- consider biking or walking if you're staying nearby. The market is dog-friendly but can get crowded mid-morning.

Seasonal Harvest Calendar

June - Early Summer

Strawberries, early cherries, asparagus, lettuce, and herbs arrive at stands. U-pick strawberry season begins. Orchard tours start.

July - Cherry Season

Peak cherry season! Rainier, Bing, and Lapin varieties flood the stands. Apricots, early peaches, and blueberries also available. U-pick cherries.

August - Stone Fruit Peak

Peaches, nectarines, plums, and late cherries. Tomatoes, corn, peppers, and melons. The most abundant month at fruit stands and markets.

September-October - Harvest

Apple season! Gala, Ambrosia, Honeycrisp, and more. Pears, grapes, pumpkins, and squash. Wine harvest overlaps for a complete farm experience.

U-Pick Orchards

U-pick (pick-your-own) orchards offer one of the most authentic Okanagan experiences. Wander through rows of fruit trees and pick your own basket of cherries, peaches, or apples straight from the branch. It's a wonderful family activity and the freshest fruit you'll ever taste.

  • How it works: Pay by the pound or bucket. Farms provide picking containers and sometimes ladders for tree fruit
  • What to bring: Sunscreen, hat, water, comfortable shoes, and containers for transporting your fruit home
  • Cost: Typically $2-5 per pound depending on the fruit. Often cheaper than buying at the stand
  • Best for families: Children love the experience and it teaches them where food comes from
  • Availability: Cherries (July), peaches and apricots (August), apples (September-October)
