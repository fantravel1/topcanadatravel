About the Kettle Valley Rail Trail

The Kettle Valley Rail Trail is one of British Columbia's most iconic outdoor experiences. Originally built between 1910 and 1916 as the Kettle Valley Railway (KVR), this engineering marvel was constructed to connect the Kootenay region to the coast through some of the most rugged terrain in the Canadian Cordillera. The railway ceased operation in 1989, and the corridor was converted into a multi-use recreational trail.

The crown jewel of the trail is the Myra Canyon section near Kelowna, which features 18 wooden trestle bridges and 2 tunnels carved through solid rock. Walking or cycling across these trestles, suspended high above the canyon floor with panoramic views of the Okanagan Valley below, is an experience unlike anything else in Canada.

In 2003, the Okanagan Mountain Park wildfire destroyed 12 of the 18 trestles. A massive community-led reconstruction effort restored all the trestles by 2008, and the Myra Canyon Trestle Restoration Society continues to maintain these heritage structures. The section was designated a National Historic Site of Canada in recognition of its engineering significance.

Engineering Marvel

The Kettle Valley Railway was called "McCulloch's Wonder" after chief engineer Andrew McCulloch, who quoted Shakespeare to name stations along the route (Romeo, Juliet, Othello, Iago, Portia, Jessica, Lear). The railway was considered one of the most expensive per-mile rail lines ever built in North America due to the extreme terrain.

The Myra Canyon Experience

The Myra Canyon section runs approximately 12 kilometres from the Ruth Station trailhead to the Myra Station trailhead. Most visitors start at the Myra Station end (the more popular trailhead) and ride or walk to the Ruth Station end and back, covering 24 km total.

The trail follows the original railway grade, which means it is remarkably flat and gentle -- railways cannot handle steep grades. This makes it accessible to casual cyclists, families with children, and walkers of all fitness levels. The gravel surface is well-maintained and suitable for hybrid or mountain bikes.

What You'll See

  • 18 Trestle Bridges: Wooden bridges spanning deep gorges, some reaching heights of 55 metres above the canyon floor
  • 2 Rock Tunnels: Hand-carved through solid rock, cool and atmospheric on hot summer days
  • Canyon Views: Breathtaking panoramas of Myra Creek canyon, Okanagan Lake, and the surrounding mountains
  • Wildflower Meadows: Stunning displays of native wildflowers from June through August
  • Wildlife: Watch for deer, black bears (at a distance), marmots, and a variety of birds including hawks and eagles
  • Heritage Markers: Interpretive signs explaining the railway's history and the 2003 fire restoration

Trail Tips

Bring water, snacks, and a light jacket -- temperatures at the canyon elevation (1,200m) can be 5-10 degrees cooler than downtown Kelowna. The access road is gravel and can be rough; a vehicle with good clearance is recommended. There are no services at the trailheads -- just vault toilets and parking.

How to Experience It

Cycling (Most Popular)

The flat railway grade is perfect for cycling. Bring your own bike or rent in Kelowna. The 24 km return trip takes 2-3 hours with stops for photos. E-bikes are welcome on the trail.

Hiking / Walking

Walk all or part of the trail. Many visitors walk 3-4 trestles from the Myra Station trailhead and return. Allow 4-6 hours for the full return trip on foot.

Guided Tours

Several Kelowna operators offer guided cycling tours including bike rental and shuttle service. Tours run $80-150 per person and include historical commentary.

Photography

Early morning and late afternoon offer the best light for photographing the trestles. The tunnels create dramatic framing opportunities. Fall colours add spectacular scenery.

Getting There

From downtown Kelowna, take KLO Road east to McCulloch Road, then follow signs to Myra Forest Service Road. The trailhead is approximately 24 km from downtown and takes about 35-40 minutes to reach. The last 8.5 km is on a gravel forest service road -- drive carefully and watch for logging trucks on weekdays.

Parking

Free parking is available at both the Myra Station and Ruth Station trailheads. The Myra Station lot is larger and more popular. On busy summer weekends, arrive before 10 AM to secure a spot.
