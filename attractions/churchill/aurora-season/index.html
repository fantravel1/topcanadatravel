Aurora Borealis in Churchill

Churchill sits directly beneath the auroral oval ‚Äî the ring of aurora activity that encircles the magnetic North Pole. This prime geographic position means the Northern Lights are not just occasional visitors here; they are a near-nightly occurrence. With over 300 nights of aurora activity per year, Churchill is statistically one of the best places on Earth to witness this celestial phenomenon.

What sets Churchill apart from other aurora destinations is the combination of factors that create ideal viewing conditions. The town is far from any major light pollution source. The flat tundra landscape provides unobstructed horizon-to-horizon views. And Churchill's position beneath the auroral oval means the lights appear directly overhead rather than on the distant horizon ‚Äî resulting in more vivid, dramatic, and immersive displays.

During the peak winter months of February and March, the aurora can appear in extraordinary forms ‚Äî sweeping curtains of green and purple, pulsating bands of light, and occasionally the rare red and blue displays that indicate particularly strong solar activity. On an active night, the entire sky can come alive with dancing light, creating a spectacle that photographs cannot fully capture.

Why February-March?

While Churchill experiences aurora activity year-round, February and March offer the best viewing combination: long dark nights (15+ hours of darkness), statistically higher solar activity around the equinoxes, and slightly warmer temperatures than the extreme cold of December-January. Clear skies are also more common in late winter.

Churchill Northern Studies Centre

The Churchill Northern Studies Centre (CNSC) is the premier aurora viewing facility in the region. Located 23 kilometres east of Churchill on the shores of Hudson Bay, the CNSC sits in complete darkness ‚Äî far from even Churchill's modest light pollution. The centre was established as a research station and has evolved into one of Canada's top destinations for aurora tourism.

Heated Observation Domes

The CNSC features specially designed heated observation domes that allow visitors to watch the aurora in comfort. These transparent domes provide panoramic sky views while protecting you from the extreme cold outside. You can spend hours watching the lights dance overhead without freezing ‚Äî a luxury that makes Churchill's aurora experience accessible to everyone, not just the hardiest winter adventurers.

Learning Vacations

The CNSC offers multi-day "Learning Vacation" packages that combine aurora viewing with daytime educational programs. Expert guides lead sessions on aurora science, Arctic ecology, Indigenous culture, and the unique natural history of the Hudson Bay region. These programs provide depth and context that transform a simple viewing trip into a comprehensive Arctic education.

Booking Tip

The CNSC's Learning Vacations for February and March are popular and often sell out months in advance. Book 6-12 months ahead for the best selection of dates. Multi-night stays increase your chances of witnessing a spectacular display, as aurora activity varies from night to night.

Winter Activities to Combine

Dog Sledding

Race across the frozen tundra behind a team of enthusiastic huskies. Half-day and full-day excursions available. An exhilarating daytime complement to evening aurora viewing. From $250+.

Igloo Building

Learn traditional Inuit snow shelter construction from expert guides. Build your own igloo on the frozen landscape and understand how Indigenous peoples have thrived in the Arctic for millennia.

Winter Wildlife Tracking

Guided snowshoe or snowmobile excursions to track Arctic foxes, ptarmigan, and other winter wildlife. Polar bears are occasionally spotted in winter too, though less reliably than in autumn.

Cultural Experiences

Visit the Itsanitaq Museum for Inuit carvings and artifacts, explore the historic Prince of Wales Fort ruins (when accessible), and learn about the Dene and Cree cultures that have called this region home for thousands of years.

Aurora Photography Guide

Churchill is a photographer's dream for aurora images. The flat tundra, frozen Hudson Bay, and occasional snow-covered foreground elements create stunning compositions.

Camera Settings

  • Manual mode: Full manual control is essential for aurora photography
  • Wide aperture: f/2.8 or wider ‚Äî the fastest lens you have
  • High ISO: Start at ISO 1600-3200 and adjust based on aurora brightness
  • Long exposure: 8-25 seconds depending on aurora movement speed
  • Wide angle lens: 14-24mm captures the full sweep of the aurora overhead
  • Manual focus: Pre-focus on a distant light or star, then tape the focus ring

Essential Gear

  • Sturdy tripod: Non-negotiable for long exposures in wind
  • Extra batteries: Cold drains batteries extremely fast ‚Äî keep spares warm inside your jacket
  • Remote shutter release: Prevents camera shake during long exposures
  • Lens warmer: Prevents frost buildup on the front element
  • Hand warmers: Essential for operating camera controls with bare fingers in -30¬∞C

Pro Tip: Time-Lapse

Set up a time-lapse sequence with an intervalometer. The aurora's movement is often more dramatic when compressed into a few seconds of video. Shoot one frame every 5-10 seconds over several hours for spectacular results. The heated domes at the CNSC allow you to monitor your camera through the glass while staying warm.

Planning Your Visit

Getting to Churchill

Fly from Winnipeg (2 hours) via Calm Air or Canadian North. VIA Rail's winter train service is also available (2 days) and offers its own stunning scenery. Most aurora tour packages include return flights from Winnipeg.

What to Pack

  • Extreme cold gear: Temperatures of -25¬∞C to -35¬∞C are normal. Full Arctic layering system required
  • Insulated boots: Rated to -40¬∞C minimum ‚Äî you will be standing outside
  • Balaclava and neck gaiter: Exposed skin freezes in minutes at these temperatures
  • Heavy mittens over liner gloves: Allows you to operate camera controls briefly with liners
  • Camera gear: See photography section above. Bring more batteries than you think you need
  • Hand and toe warmers: Pack a large supply ‚Äî you will use many per night

Dark Sky Location

Churchill has virtually zero light pollution compared to southern cities. Your eyes will fully dark-adapt within 20-30 minutes, revealing not just the aurora but an incredible canopy of stars, the Milky Way, and occasionally even the zodiacal light. Even on nights when the aurora is quiet, the night sky alone is worth the trip.
