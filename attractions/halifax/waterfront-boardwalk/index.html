About the Waterfront

The Halifax Waterfront Boardwalk is the beating heart of Nova Scotia's capital, a vibrant 4-kilometre wooden walkway that winds along the shores of one of the world's great natural harbours. From the Casino Nova Scotia in the north to the Canadian Museum of Immigration at Pier 21 in the south, this beloved promenade showcases the best of Halifax's maritime heritage and contemporary culture.

Halifax Harbour itself is remarkable - the second-largest natural harbour in the world after Sydney, Australia. Deep enough for the largest container ships and ice-free year-round, it has been the foundation of the city's importance since Edward Cornwallis founded Halifax in 1749 as a British naval base.

Today, the waterfront pulses with energy. Street performers entertain crowds, buskers play Maritime folk songs, and the air carries the scent of fresh seafood from the many restaurants and food trucks. Historic tall ships bob alongside modern ferries, creating a perfect blend of past and present.

The Dartmouth Ferry

The Halifax-Dartmouth ferry is the oldest saltwater ferry service in North America, operating continuously since 1752. For just $2.75, you get a mini harbour cruise with stunning views of the Halifax skyline. It's both a transit system and a tourist attraction - don't miss it!

What to See & Do

Historic Properties

This collection of 19th-century stone warehouses now houses restaurants, pubs, and boutiques. The buildings once stored goods from Halifax's busy shipping trade and are among the oldest waterfront structures in North America.

Tall Ships & Vessels

Various historic vessels are often docked along the waterfront, including the CSS Acadia at the Maritime Museum. During summer, you might see the Bluenose II or visiting tall ships that offer tours and sails.

Bishop's Landing

An upscale waterfront complex featuring specialty shops, restaurants, and the Bishops Cellar wine boutique. Great for evening dining with harbour views and people watching.

Harbour Hopper Tours

These amphibious vehicles tour the streets of Halifax before splashing into the harbour for water views. Fun for families and a unique way to see the city from both land and sea.

Museums on the Waterfront

  • Maritime Museum of the Atlantic: Largest collection of Titanic artifacts, Halifax Explosion exhibit, and the story of the Bluenose
  • Canadian Museum of Immigration at Pier 21: The "Ellis Island of Canada" where one million immigrants arrived between 1928-1971
  • Discovery Centre: Interactive science centre perfect for families with children

Street Performers & Entertainment

During summer months, the boardwalk comes alive with licensed buskers performing everything from Celtic fiddle music to acrobatics and magic shows. Peak performance times are typically late morning through early evening. The Halifax Busker Festival in August brings world-class street performers from around the globe.

Walking the Boardwalk

The boardwalk naturally divides into distinct sections, each with its own character. Here's what you'll find from north to south:

Northern Section (Casino to Historic Properties)

  • Casino Nova Scotia and Harbourfront Marriott
  • Wave sculpture and seating areas
  • Halifax Ferry Terminal (boats to Dartmouth)
  • Historic Properties shops and restaurants
  • Privateer's Warehouse - oldest building on the waterfront

Central Section (Maritime Museum Area)

  • Maritime Museum of the Atlantic
  • CSS Acadia - historic hydrographic survey ship
  • HMCS Sackville - last surviving WWII corvette
  • Art Gallery of Nova Scotia nearby
  • Bishop's Landing dining and shopping

Southern Section (Seaport District to Pier 21)

  • Seaport Farmers' Market (Saturdays)
  • Discovery Centre
  • Cruise ship terminal
  • Canadian Museum of Immigration at Pier 21

Start Your Walk Here

For the best experience, start at the Ferry Terminal (central location) and walk south toward Pier 21, then loop back. This puts the sun behind you in the morning for better photos and saves the busiest section (Historic Properties) for later when performers are active.

Dining on the Waterfront

The waterfront offers some of Halifax's best dining, from casual fish and chips to upscale seafood restaurants. Fresh catches from the Atlantic are the specialty here.

Must-Try Foods

  • Lobster Roll: Atlantic lobster on a buttered, toasted roll - a Maritime classic
  • Fish and Chips: Traditionally made with haddock in Nova Scotia
  • Seafood Chowder: Creamy and loaded with lobster, scallops, and haddock
  • Donairs: Halifax's official food - spiced beef with sweet sauce in pita
  • Digby Scallops: Famous large scallops from nearby Digby, pan-seared or bacon-wrapped

Popular Waterfront Restaurants

  • Salty's: Classic waterfront dining with harbour views and fresh seafood
  • The Bicycle Thief: Italian-inspired with excellent cocktails at Bishop's Landing
  • Murphy's: Traditional fish and chips and casual Maritime fare
  • Lower Deck: Pub atmosphere with live Maritime music nightly

Practical Tips

  • Best Time to Visit: Weekday mornings for fewer crowds; evenings for dining and atmosphere
  • Cruise Ship Days: The waterfront gets very busy when cruise ships are in port - check the schedule if you want to avoid or experience the buzz
  • Parking: Use the Salter Street or Lower Water Street parking lots. Street parking is limited and time-restricted
  • Weather: The harbour creates its own microclimate - bring a light jacket even on warm days
  • Accessibility: The boardwalk is fully accessible with ramps and smooth surfaces throughout
  • Combine Attractions: Plan to visit the Maritime Museum and take the Dartmouth ferry for a full waterfront experience
  • Farmers' Market: Visit on Saturday morning for the Halifax Seaport Farmers' Market - Atlantic Canada's largest
