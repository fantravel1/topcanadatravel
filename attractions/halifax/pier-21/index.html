About Pier 21

Pier 21 holds a special place in Canadian hearts. Between 1928 and 1971, this oceanfront facility served as the gateway to Canada for nearly one million immigrants arriving by sea. Today, one in five Canadians can trace their family's arrival to this historic pier, making it one of the most significant heritage sites in the country.

Designated a National Historic Site in 1997 and opened as the Canadian Museum of Immigration in 1999, Pier 21 tells the powerful stories of those who left everything behind for the promise of a new life. From families fleeing war-torn Europe to war brides crossing the Atlantic to join their Canadian husbands, each story is a thread in the tapestry of modern Canada.

The museum preserves the original immigration hall where newcomers took their first steps on Canadian soil, anxiously awaiting their turn to be processed, examined, and - hopefully - admitted to their new homeland. Walking through these halls, you can almost hear the babel of languages and feel the mix of hope and fear that filled this space.

One in Five Canadians

An estimated 20% of Canadians have a family connection to Pier 21. If your ancestors immigrated to Canada between 1928 and 1971, there's a good chance they walked through these very halls. The museum's research centre can help you trace their journey.

Who Came Through Pier 21?

European Immigrants

Hundreds of thousands fled economic hardship, political persecution, and war in Europe. Poles, Ukrainians, Italians, Dutch, Germans, and many others arrived seeking opportunity in Canada's growing cities and farms.

War Brides

After WWII, nearly 48,000 women who had married Canadian servicemen overseas arrived with their approximately 22,000 children. These "war brides" came primarily from Britain but also from the Netherlands, Belgium, and France.

Hungarian Refugees

Following the failed 1956 Hungarian Revolution, Canada opened its doors to 37,000 refugees. Many arrived at Pier 21, fleeing Soviet oppression for freedom in Canada.

Displaced Persons

After WWII, over 165,000 "DPs" - people left homeless by the war - came to Canada through Pier 21. Many were survivors of concentration camps or had lost everything to the conflict.

Other Significant Groups

  • British Home Children: Over 100,000 poor and orphaned British children were sent to Canada between 1869-1948 to work as farm laborers and domestic servants
  • Canadian Troops: 368,000 Canadian military personnel departed from Pier 21 during WWII, many never to return
  • Evacuee Children: During WWII, British children were evacuated to Canada for safety from the Blitz
  • Post-War Refugees: Czechs, Ugandan Asians, and others fleeing persecution throughout the mid-20th century

Museum Exhibits

The museum occupies the original immigration shed and offers both permanent and rotating exhibits that bring the immigration experience to life.

Permanent Galleries

  • Canadian Immigration Hall: Walk through the original processing area where immigrants were interviewed, examined, and either admitted or - heartbreakingly - turned away
  • Pier 21 Story: The history of this building from its construction through its years as Canada's immigration gateway
  • Ship Displays: Models and artifacts from the ocean liners that brought immigrants across the Atlantic
  • Oral History Theatre: Listen to recorded testimonies from those who actually passed through Pier 21
  • Scotiabank Family History Centre: Research your own family's immigration story

Before Your Visit

If you think your family may have come through Pier 21, search the online database at pier21.ca before your visit. Knowing your ancestor's ship name and arrival date will help staff locate records and make your research visit more productive.

The Immigration Experience

Arriving at Pier 21 was just the first step in a complex and often stressful process. The museum recreates what newcomers faced after weeks or months at sea:

The Process

  • Disembarkation: After weeks at sea, often in crowded conditions, immigrants walked down the gangplank into the cavernous immigration hall
  • Documentation Check: Officials verified travel documents and questioned each arrival about their background, skills, and sponsors
  • Medical Examination: Doctors checked for communicable diseases - failing this exam could mean deportation
  • Customs Inspection: Baggage was searched for prohibited items
  • Railway Tickets: Most immigrants boarded trains right from Pier 21, heading to destinations across Canada
  • First Canadian Meal: Many received their first Canadian food here - a memorable moment often mentioned in oral histories

For some, the process took hours. For others - those with incomplete papers or health concerns - it stretched into days of anxious waiting. The museum doesn't shy away from the difficult aspects: the families separated, the individuals turned away, the dreams deferred or destroyed.

Family History Research

The Scotiabank Family History Centre at Pier 21 is a treasure trove for genealogists. Staff can help you search:

  • Ship Manifests: Lists of passengers arriving on specific vessels
  • Immigration Records: Official documentation of arrivals
  • Oral History Collection: Over 4,500 recorded testimonies from immigrants
  • War Bride Records: Special documentation for the war bride program
  • Photo Archives: Images of ships, the pier, and immigrants

Research Tip

Come prepared with as much information as possible: full names, approximate dates, country of origin, and ship name if known. Even partial information can help researchers find your family's records. Consider booking a research appointment in advance for dedicated staff assistance.

Planning Your Visit

  • Allow 1.5-2.5 Hours: The exhibits are emotionally engaging and deserve time to absorb
  • Combine with Waterfront: Pier 21 is at the southern end of the boardwalk - walk from downtown
  • Cruise Ship Terminal: The building also serves as Halifax's cruise terminal - expect extra activity on cruise days
  • Book Research Time: If you're searching family records, consider booking a research appointment in advance
  • Bring Tissues: The oral histories and personal stories can be deeply moving

Getting There

Pier 21 is located at the southern end of the Halifax waterfront at 1055 Marginal Road. It's a 20-minute walk from the Maritime Museum along the boardwalk, or you can drive and use the adjacent parking lot. The museum is fully accessible.
