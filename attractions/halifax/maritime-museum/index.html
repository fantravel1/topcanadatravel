About the Museum

The Maritime Museum of the Atlantic is Nova Scotia's oldest and largest maritime museum, and one of the finest in Canada. Located on the historic Halifax waterfront, it tells the dramatic stories of the sea and the people who have lived, worked, and died upon it. With over 30,000 artifacts in its collection, the museum brings to life the region's deep connection to the Atlantic Ocean.

The museum occupies a historic chandlery building (where ships once purchased their supplies) on the working waterfront, with two significant vessels - the CSS Acadia and HMCS Sackville - moored at its wharves. This living connection to maritime history, combined with world-class exhibits, makes it an essential Halifax experience.

Three stories dominate the museum's narrative: the sinking of the Titanic, the devastating Halifax Explosion of 1917, and the legendary racing schooner Bluenose. Each is told through compelling artifacts, personal stories, and immersive exhibits that bring these pivotal moments in maritime history vividly to life.

Halifax's Titanic Connection

When the Titanic sank on April 15, 1912, Halifax was the nearest major port. Ships from Halifax recovered over 300 victims, and many were buried in Halifax cemeteries. The museum has the world's largest collection of wooden artifacts from the ship, including a deck chair, door frames, and personal effects recovered from the site.

Major Exhibits

Titanic: The Unsinkable Ship

The world's largest collection of wooden Titanic artifacts, including a deck chair, oak trim, and items belonging to passengers. Halifax's unique role in the aftermath of the disaster is movingly told.

Halifax Explosion

On December 6, 1917, a collision in the harbour caused the largest man-made explosion before the atomic bomb. The exhibit includes artifacts from the disaster and stories of survival and heroism.

Shipwreck Treasures

Nova Scotia's "Graveyard of the Atlantic" has claimed thousands of ships. See gold coins, navigational instruments, and cargo recovered from wrecks dating back centuries.

Days of Sail

Celebrates the age when Nova Scotia was a world shipbuilding powerhouse. Learn about the Bluenose, the famous racing schooner that appears on the Canadian dime and never lost a race.

Additional Galleries

  • Small Craft Gallery: Over 70 traditional boats showcasing regional boatbuilding traditions
  • Age of Steam: The transition from sail to steam power, including a working steam engine
  • Navy Gallery: Canadian naval history from World War I to modern peacekeeping missions
  • Hydrography: The science of charting the seas, featuring equipment from the CSS Acadia
  • Theodore Too: The beloved tugboat from the children's TV show "Theodore Tugboat" (seasonal)

Historic Vessels

CSS Acadia

Moored at the museum's wharf, the CSS Acadia is the last surviving ship to have served in the Canadian wartime navy in both World Wars. Built in 1913, she spent most of her career as a hydrographic survey vessel, charting Canada's coastlines. Visitors can tour the ship's bridge, engine room, and crew quarters, experiencing what life was like aboard a working research vessel.

HMCS Sackville

The last surviving corvette of the 123 that served in the Royal Canadian Navy during World War II. These small, tough ships were the workhorses of the Battle of the Atlantic, escorting convoys through U-boat-infested waters. HMCS Sackville is now Canada's Naval Memorial and is open for guided tours during summer months.

Ship Touring Tip

The CSS Acadia is included with museum admission and is a highlight for many visitors. The HMCS Sackville is separately operated and may have different hours or require a small additional donation. Check current schedules at the museum entrance.

The Halifax Explosion

On the morning of December 6, 1917, the French cargo ship Mont-Blanc, fully loaded with wartime explosives, collided with the Norwegian vessel Imo in Halifax Harbour. The resulting fire drew curious crowds to the waterfront before the Mont-Blanc exploded at 9:04 AM.

The explosion was the largest man-made blast before the nuclear age, with a force equivalent to roughly 2.9 kilotons of TNT. Nearly 2,000 people were killed instantly, 9,000 were injured (many blinded by flying glass), and 25,000 were left homeless. The entire north end of Halifax was flattened.

The museum's exhibit includes recovered artifacts, personal stories of survivors, and explains the massive relief effort - including the annual Christmas tree Nova Scotia sends to Boston in gratitude for their immediate assistance. The exhibit is both heartbreaking and inspiring in its telling of tragedy and resilience.

Planning Your Visit

  • Allow 2-3 Hours: The museum is extensive and the exhibits deserve time to absorb
  • Start with Titanic: The most popular exhibit - arrive early or late to avoid cruise ship crowds
  • Don't Miss the Ships: The CSS Acadia is included in admission and shouldn't be skipped
  • Check for Programs: The museum offers special talks, films, and children's programs
  • Visit the Gift Shop: One of Halifax's best museum shops with maritime books and unique gifts
  • Combine with Waterfront: The museum is on the boardwalk - plan time to explore before or after

Getting There

The museum is located on the Halifax waterfront at 1675 Lower Water Street. It's easily reached on foot from downtown or along the boardwalk from the Historic Properties. Paid parking is available in nearby lots. The museum is fully accessible.
