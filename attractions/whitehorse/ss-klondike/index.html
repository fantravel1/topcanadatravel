About the SS Klondike

The SS Klondike is a beautifully restored sternwheeler paddle steamer and a National Historic Site of Canada, permanently dry-docked on the banks of the Yukon River in downtown Whitehorse. Built in 1937, it was the largest vessel to operate on the upper Yukon River, carrying passengers and freight between Whitehorse and Dawson City for nearly two decades.

The original SS Klondike (known as SS Klondike I) was built in 1929 but sank in 1936 after hitting a rock. The current vessel, SS Klondike II, was constructed the following year using salvaged engines and materials from the original ship. It served the Yukon River route from 1937 until 1955, when improved road access made river transportation obsolete.

Parks Canada meticulously restored the vessel to its 1940s appearance, and today visitors can explore the ship from stem to stern, walking through the cargo hold, engine room, passenger cabins, dining saloon, and wheelhouse. The guided tour brings to life the era when sternwheelers were the lifeline of Yukon communities, carrying everything from mail and supplies to hopeful gold seekers.

The Yukon River Route

The SS Klondike made the 740-kilometre downstream journey from Whitehorse to Dawson City in just 36 hours. The return trip upstream, fighting the powerful Yukon River current, took a gruelling 5 days. The ship could carry 300 tons of cargo and over 60 passengers, making it a vital link for communities along the river that had no road access.

The Guided Tour

Parks Canada offers guided tours of the SS Klondike during summer season, typically from mid-May to early September. Knowledgeable interpreters lead visitors through the ship, sharing stories of life on the Yukon River and the role sternwheelers played in the development of Canada's North.

Tour Highlights

  • Cargo Hold: See how supplies were loaded and stored for the journey to Dawson City
  • Engine Room: Explore the massive wood-fired steam engines that powered the paddlewheel
  • Passenger Cabins: Experience the first-class and economy accommodations that passengers enjoyed
  • Dining Saloon: Step into the elegant dining room where passengers ate during the voyage
  • Wheelhouse: See where the captain navigated the treacherous Yukon River
  • Paddlewheel: View the massive stern paddlewheel that propelled the 64-metre vessel

Visitor Tips

Arrive 10-15 minutes before the scheduled tour time. Tours run on a set schedule throughout the day. The ship has narrow passageways and steep stairs, so wear comfortable shoes. Photography is welcome throughout the vessel. Check Parks Canada website for current tour times and seasonal opening dates.

Gold Rush Transportation History

Sternwheelers were the backbone of Yukon transportation from the 1898 Klondike Gold Rush through the mid-20th century. Before roads were built, the Yukon River was the primary highway connecting communities across the vast territory.

At the height of the Gold Rush, dozens of sternwheelers plied the Yukon River, carrying tens of thousands of gold seekers, supplies, and mail. The British Yukon Navigation Company, which operated the SS Klondike, ran a fleet of vessels that connected with the White Pass & Yukon Route Railway in Whitehorse, creating a transportation corridor from the Pacific coast to the goldfields.

Gold Rush Era (1898-1900)

Over 100,000 people set out for the Klondike goldfields. Sternwheelers became the fastest way to travel the final stretch from Whitehorse to Dawson City, replacing dangerous boat runs through Miles Canyon.

Working Years (1937-1955)

The SS Klondike served as freight and passenger vessel for 18 years. It was the pride of the British Yukon Navigation fleet, making regular runs throughout the navigation season.

Retirement (1955)

The completion of the Klondike Highway made river transportation obsolete. The SS Klondike made its final voyage and was donated to the Canadian government for preservation.

Restoration (1966-present)

Parks Canada moved the vessel to its current location and undertook extensive restoration. It was designated a National Historic Site and opened to the public as a museum ship.

Getting There

The SS Klondike is located along the Yukon River near the Robert Campbell Bridge in downtown Whitehorse. It is easily accessible on foot from the city centre, approximately a 10-minute walk along the river trail from Main Street. Look for the large white vessel on the riverbank.

Free parking is available near the site. The SS Klondike is also a stop on the Whitehorse Waterfront Trolley during summer months, providing a scenic way to arrive.
