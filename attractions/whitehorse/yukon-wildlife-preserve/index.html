About the Yukon Wildlife Preserve

The Yukon Wildlife Preserve is a 700-acre facility dedicated to the conservation and public education of northern Canadian wildlife. Located 25 kilometres from downtown Whitehorse, the preserve provides spacious, natural habitats for 13 species of animals that are native to the Yukon and northern Canada.

Unlike a traditional zoo, the Yukon Wildlife Preserve gives its animals large enclosures that closely resemble their natural environments. The rolling terrain of boreal forest, meadows, and wetlands allows animals to behave naturally, making this one of the best places to observe and photograph northern wildlife in a setting that feels authentic.

The preserve serves an important conservation role, participating in breeding programs for endangered and vulnerable species, rehabilitating injured wildlife, and educating the public about northern ecosystems and the challenges facing Yukon's wildlife.

Conservation Mission

The Yukon Wildlife Preserve operates as a non-profit organization dedicated to the conservation of northern wildlife. Revenue from admissions and tours supports animal care, habitat maintenance, breeding programs, and wildlife education. Many of the animals at the preserve are rescues that cannot be returned to the wild.

Animals You Can See

The preserve is home to 13 species of northern wildlife, each living in spacious habitats designed to mimic their natural environments.

Wood Bison

Canada's largest land animal, weighing up to 900 kg. The preserve's herd roams a large meadow habitat. These iconic animals nearly went extinct but have been recovered through conservation efforts.

Canadian Lynx

The elusive boreal cat with distinctive ear tufts and large snowshoe-like paws. Rarely seen in the wild, the preserve offers one of the best opportunities to observe this beautiful predator.

Woodland Caribou

Both male and female caribou grow antlers. The preserve's caribou are part of a species that migrates vast distances in the wild, and both sexes can be seen with their impressive racks.

Musk Ox

Ancient Arctic survivors with shaggy coats of qiviut (the warmest natural fibre). These prehistoric-looking animals have been on Earth for over 100,000 years and are one of the preserve's most popular residents.

Mountain Goats

Sure-footed climbers with thick white coats. Watch them navigate rocky terrain with incredible agility. Their habitat at the preserve includes rocky outcrops that simulate their alpine environment.

More Species

Also home to thin-horn sheep, elk, mule deer, moose, Arctic fox, red fox, snowy owl, and peregrine falcon. Each species has its own dedicated habitat area along the walking trail.

Visiting Options

Walking Trail (Self-Guided)

The 5 km interpretive walking trail loops through the preserve, passing each animal habitat. Interpretive signs provide information about each species, their ecology, and conservation status. The trail is mostly flat and accessible, though it can be muddy in spring and snowy in winter. Allow 2-3 hours to complete the full loop at a leisurely pace.

Guided Bus Tour

For those who prefer not to walk the full trail, guided bus tours are available at an additional cost. Knowledgeable guides drive you through the preserve, stopping at each habitat for viewing and photography. The bus tour takes approximately 1.5 hours and provides expert commentary on each species.

Photography Tips

Bring a telephoto lens (200mm+) for close-up wildlife shots. Early morning and late afternoon offer the best light and most active animals. In winter, animals are often more visible against the snow. The preserve allows tripods on the walking trail. Some enclosures have mesh fencing, so position your lens close to the fence for clear shots.

Best Times to Visit

Summer (June-August)

Long daylight hours, babies and juveniles visible, lush green habitats. Warmest walking conditions. This is the busiest season but also the most rewarding for viewing active animals.

Fall (September-October)

Elk and caribou in rut with full antlers. Stunning fall colours in the boreal forest. Fewer visitors and cooler, comfortable temperatures for the walking trail.

Winter (November-March)

Animals in full winter coats. Bison and musk ox are particularly photogenic against the snow. Cross-country skiing may be available on the trail. A unique and quiet experience.

Spring (April-May)

Baby animals begin appearing. Migrating birds return. Snow melts and the landscape begins to green. A hopeful and renewing time to visit the preserve.

Getting There

The Yukon Wildlife Preserve is located 25 km north of downtown Whitehorse on the Takhini Hot Springs Road. The drive takes approximately 20-25 minutes. Follow the Alaska Highway north and turn left onto Takhini Hot Springs Road, then follow signs to the preserve.

The preserve can be easily combined with a visit to Takhini Hot Springs, which is located further along the same road. There is no public transit to the preserve, so a car or tour shuttle is required.
