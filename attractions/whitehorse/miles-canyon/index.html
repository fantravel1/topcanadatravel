About Miles Canyon

Miles Canyon is a dramatic basalt canyon carved by the Yukon River, located just 10 kilometres south of downtown Whitehorse. The canyon walls rise steeply on both sides of the turquoise-green river, creating one of the most photographed natural landmarks in the Yukon Territory.

The canyon was formed over thousands of years as the Yukon River cut through volcanic basalt rock, leaving dramatic columnar formations and sheer cliff faces. The water here once raged through dangerous rapids that were feared by Klondike Gold Rush stampeders, though the construction of the Whitehorse Dam in 1958 tamed the river to its current calm state.

Today, a graceful suspension bridge spans the canyon, providing spectacular views of the turquoise water below and the columnar basalt walls on either side. Well-maintained hiking trails follow both rims of the canyon through boreal forest of spruce and aspen, offering multiple viewpoints and interpretive signs about the area's natural and human history.

Klondike Gold Rush History

During the 1898 Klondike Gold Rush, Miles Canyon was one of the most dangerous obstacles on the route to the Dawson City goldfields. Thousands of stampeders had to navigate their hand-built boats through violent rapids that claimed many lives and destroyed countless vessels. The North-West Mounted Police eventually stationed officers here to inspect boats and required inexperienced boatmen to hire professional pilots. A wooden tramway was built along the canyon rim to portage supplies around the rapids.

Hiking Trails

Miles Canyon offers a network of well-maintained trails suitable for hikers of all abilities. The trails wind through boreal forest along the canyon rim, with regular viewpoints overlooking the river and canyon walls.

Canyon Rim Trail (Main Loop)

The most popular route follows both sides of the canyon, crossing the suspension bridge. The full loop is approximately 5 km and takes 1.5 to 2.5 hours depending on your pace and how long you linger at viewpoints. The trail is well-marked with interpretive signs about the geology, ecology, and Gold Rush history of the area.

Shorter Walks

  • Bridge Viewpoint: A short 15-minute walk from the parking area to the suspension bridge for canyon views (30 min return)
  • East Rim Trail: Follow the east side of the canyon for 2 km to several excellent viewpoints
  • West Rim Trail: Connects to the Millennium Trail system leading back to Whitehorse

Hiking Tips

Wear sturdy footwear as some sections are rocky. Bring water and snacks. In summer, bug spray is essential as mosquitoes can be thick near the river. Stay on marked trails and behind fences at viewpoints, as the canyon edges can be steep and unstable. Dogs are welcome on leash.

Best Times to Visit

Summer (June-August)

Best hiking conditions with long daylight hours. The turquoise water is at its most vivid. Wildflowers along the trail. Warmest temperatures for leisurely exploration.

Fall (September)

Spectacular autumn colours as aspen and birch turn gold against the dark spruce. Fewer visitors and cooler, comfortable hiking temperatures. Excellent photography conditions.

Winter

The canyon transforms with ice and snow. Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing are possible on some trails. A completely different and quieter experience.

Spring (May)

Snow melts and trails reopen. Spring runoff can increase water levels. Wildlife is active and migratory birds return. A quieter time before summer tourists arrive.

Getting There

Miles Canyon is located approximately 10 km south of downtown Whitehorse. From the city centre, head south on the Alaska Highway (South Access Road) and follow signs to Miles Canyon Road. The trailhead parking lot is at the end of the road. The drive takes about 15 minutes.

Cyclists can reach Miles Canyon via the Millennium Trail, a paved pathway that connects downtown Whitehorse to the canyon. The ride is approximately 8 km and is a pleasant, mostly flat route along the river.
