About Northern Lights in Whitehorse

Whitehorse sits directly beneath the auroral oval at 60.7 degrees north latitude, placing it in one of the most reliable aurora viewing corridors on Earth. With over 300 nights of potential aurora activity per year and some of the darkest skies in North America, the Yukon capital has become a world-class destination for Northern Lights seekers.

Unlike other popular aurora destinations, Whitehorse offers a unique combination of accessibility, low light pollution, and relatively mild winter temperatures for a northern location. The city's position in a wide river valley provides expansive views of the sky, while the surrounding wilderness ensures dark horizons in every direction.

The aurora borealis appears when charged particles from the sun interact with gases in Earth's atmosphere, creating curtains, arcs, and spirals of green, purple, pink, and red light that dance across the sky. On active nights, the display can last for hours, filling the entire sky with shimmering colour.

Northern Lights Resort & Spa

The Northern Lights Resort & Spa, located outside Whitehorse, offers a dedicated aurora viewing experience with heated cabins, wake-up calls when the lights appear, and photography equipment. It is one of the most popular accommodations for aurora seekers visiting the Yukon.

Guided Aurora Tours

Guided Northern Lights tours are the most popular way to experience the aurora in Whitehorse. Professional guides transport you to optimal dark-sky locations away from the city, provide warm drinks, set up heated viewing shelters, and offer photography instruction to help you capture the experience.

Most tours operate from September through March and run from approximately 10 PM to 2-3 AM. Tour operators monitor solar activity and weather forecasts to choose the best viewing locations each night. Many tours include DSLR camera setups with tripods so guests can take professional-quality aurora photos.

What Tours Include

  • Transportation: Hotel pickup and return, driving 20-45 minutes to dark-sky locations
  • Heated Shelters: Warm-up cabins or heated tents at viewing sites so you can stay comfortable
  • Photography Guidance: Camera settings, tripod use, and tips for capturing the aurora on camera
  • Hot Beverages: Hot chocolate, tea, and coffee to keep you warm during the wait
  • Winter Gear: Some operators provide warm parkas and boots if needed

Photography Tips

For the best aurora photos, use a camera with manual settings. Set ISO to 1600-3200, aperture to f/2.8 or wider, and shutter speed to 8-15 seconds. A sturdy tripod is essential. Many tour operators provide camera equipment and instruction for guests.

Best Times for Aurora Viewing

September - October

Equinox activity boosts aurora displays. Still relatively mild temperatures (-5 to 5°C). Dark skies return after summer midnight sun. Fall colours add scenic beauty.

November - January

Longest nights with up to 18 hours of darkness. Coldest temperatures (-20 to -35°C) but very clear skies. Snow-covered landscapes create magical scenery.

February - March

Second equinox peak with strong aurora activity. Temperatures warming slightly. Excellent snow conditions for combining with winter activities like dog sledding.

Late August

Aurora season begins as nights grow dark again. Pleasant temperatures. Fewer crowds than peak winter season. Can combine with summer hiking activities.

DIY Aurora Viewing

If you prefer to hunt the aurora independently, Whitehorse offers excellent opportunities for self-guided viewing. Drive 15-30 minutes outside the city in any direction to find dark skies away from light pollution.

Top Viewing Spots

  • Takhini Hot Springs Road: Head north on the Hot Springs Road for dark skies with open views. Bonus: combine with a hot springs soak
  • Fish Lake Road: Wide-open lake views provide unobstructed horizons to the north
  • Alaska Highway South: Drive 20 minutes south toward Marsh Lake for dark skies over the water
  • Yukon River Pullouts: Several pullout areas along the river offer good northern horizons

Essential Tips for DIY Viewing

Check aurora forecasts on the University of Alaska Geophysical Institute website before heading out. Dress in layers with extreme cold weather gear in winter. Bring a thermos of hot drinks, a blanket, and a fully charged phone. Always tell someone where you are going and carry emergency supplies in your vehicle.

What to Wear & Bring

  • Winter (Nov-Mar): Heavy parka, insulated pants, winter boots rated to -40°C, multiple layers, hand warmers, balaclava, and insulated gloves
  • Shoulder Season (Aug-Oct, Apr): Warm jacket, insulated layers, warm hat and gloves, sturdy shoes
  • Camera Gear: DSLR or mirrorless camera with wide-angle lens, sturdy tripod, extra batteries (cold drains batteries fast)
  • Extras: Thermos with hot drinks, snacks, headlamp with red light mode, camping chair or blanket to sit on

Frequently Asked Questions

The aurora season runs from mid-August to mid-April. The peak months are September-October and February-March, when dark skies combine with high solar activity around the equinoxes. However, spectacular displays can occur any night during the season.

Guided aurora tours start from around $120 CAD per person. Most tours run 3-5 hours and include transport to dark sky locations, hot drinks, heated shelters, and photography guidance. Multi-night packages and lodge stays are also available at higher price points.

Strong aurora displays can sometimes be visible from the city, but light pollution reduces the experience. For the best viewing, head 15-30 minutes outside Whitehorse to dark sky locations. Guided tours handle this transportation for you.
