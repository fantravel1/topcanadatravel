Beautiful Waterfall with Iconic Fairmont Banff Springs Backdrop

About Bow Falls

Bow Falls is one of Banff's most accessible and photogenic natural attractions. This beautiful waterfall on the Bow River drops approximately 9 metres (30 feet) over a series of rocky ledges, creating a dramatic display of rushing water just a short walk from downtown Banff.

What makes Bow Falls truly special is its setting. The falls are framed by the majestic Fairmont Banff Springs hotel rising castle-like in the background, creating one of Banff's most iconic photographic compositions. The combination of natural beauty and architectural grandeur makes this one of the most photographed locations in the Canadian Rockies.

The falls were made famous in the 1953 film "River of No Return" starring Marilyn Monroe and Robert Mitchum. Several scenes were filmed at this location, cementing Bow Falls' place in Hollywood history. Today, visitors come from around the world to capture their own images of this stunning natural feature.

A Free Natural Wonder

In a national park with many paid attractions, Bow Falls stands out as a completely free experience. You only need a Banff National Park pass to enter the park itself. The falls are accessible year-round and beautiful in every season.

Best Viewpoints

Main Viewpoint (South Side)

The most popular viewpoint is accessed from the parking lot off Bow Falls Trail. A short paved path leads to viewing areas above and beside the falls. This angle captures the full breadth of the cascading water with views toward the Banff Springs hotel.

Below the Falls

Stairs and a trail lead down to the riverbank below the falls. From here, you can feel the mist on your face and experience the raw power of the rushing water. This is the closest viewpoint to the falls and offers dramatic photographs looking up at the cascades.

North Bank Trail

For a different perspective, cross to the north side of the river via the Central Park footbridge and follow the Bow River Trail. This angle offers views of the falls with Sulphur Mountain as a backdrop instead of the hotel.

Photography Tips

For the classic shot with the Fairmont Banff Springs, position yourself on the south side viewing area. A neutral density filter allows for long exposures that create silky water effects. Visit during golden hour for the warmest light on both the falls and the castle-like hotel.

Visiting Throughout the Year

Spring (May-June)

Peak water flow from mountain snowmelt creates the most dramatic falls. The rushing water is incredibly powerful, and the mist can be felt from the viewpoints. Spring offers the most impressive display.

Summer (July-August)

Warm weather makes this the most popular time to visit. Water levels are still good in early summer. Combine with a walk along the riverside trails for a perfect Banff morning.

Fall (September-October)

Golden larches and autumn colors create a beautiful contrast with the turquoise water. Fewer visitors mean more peaceful photo opportunities. Crisp mountain air enhances the experience.

Winter (November-April)

Ice formations along the falls create a winter wonderland scene. The Fairmont Banff Springs covered in snow makes for magical photographs. Bundle up‚Äîit's cold but beautiful!

Connecting Trails

Bow Falls is a natural starting or ending point for several scenic walks around Banff:

Bow River Trail

This easy, paved trail follows the Bow River from downtown Banff past the falls and continues toward the Banff Springs Golf Course. The 3 km round trip from Central Park is flat and suitable for all fitness levels, with beautiful river views throughout.

Spray River Trail

From Bow Falls, you can continue on the Spray River Trail, which loops around the Fairmont Banff Springs and follows the Spray River. This 12 km loop offers excellent chances to spot wildlife and experience the quiet forest away from town.

Tunnel Mountain Trail

For a more challenging option, combine Bow Falls with a hike up Tunnel Mountain. Start or end at the falls, then climb to the 1,692 m summit for panoramic views of the town and valley. The trail is 4.3 km round trip with 260 m elevation gain.

Hollywood History

Bow Falls earned a place in Hollywood history when it appeared in the 1953 film "River of No Return," directed by Otto Preminger. The adventure film starred Marilyn Monroe as a saloon singer and Robert Mitchum as a widowed farmer, with several scenes filmed at the falls.

Marilyn Monroe herself was reportedly injured during filming when she slipped on the rocks while shooting a scene at the falls. Despite the incident, the stunning natural backdrop made the film a visual spectacle that introduced Banff's beauty to audiences worldwide.

The film connection adds a layer of nostalgia for classic movie fans who visit Bow Falls. While the landscape remains largely unchanged since 1953, the Fairmont Banff Springs hotel in the background provides continuity with the scenes captured over seven decades ago.

Getting There

On Foot (Recommended)

The most enjoyable way to reach Bow Falls is on foot from downtown Banff. Walk south on Banff Avenue, cross the Bow River Bridge, then take the first right onto Bow Falls Trail road, or follow the Bow River Trail along the riverbank. The walk takes 15-20 minutes and is scenic throughout.

By Car

Drive south on Banff Avenue, cross the bridge, and turn right onto Bow Falls Trail. A small parking lot is located near the falls viewpoint. Note: The lot is small and fills quickly during peak times. Consider walking or arriving early.

By Bike

The Bow River Trail is an excellent cycling route. Bike rentals are available in downtown Banff. The paved trail makes for an easy ride with stops at the falls and continuing to the Banff Springs hotel.

Roam Transit

Route 6 stops near the falls, making it accessible without a car. Check current schedules as service may be seasonal.

Frequently Asked Questions

Most visitors spend 15-30 minutes at Bow Falls, which is enough time to view the falls from multiple angles and take photographs. No, swimming is not permitted at Bow Falls. The water is extremely cold (glacier-fed), the currents are dangerously strong, and the rocky riverbed creates hazardous conditions. Please stay on designated trails and viewpoints for your safety. Enjoy the falls from the viewing areas only.

The upper viewpoint area near the parking lot is accessible via a paved path. However, the trails leading down to the base of the falls involve stairs and uneven terrain that are not wheelchair accessible. The accessible viewpoint still offers excellent views of the falls and the Banff Springs hotel backdrop.

For photography, golden hour (early morning or late afternoon) offers the best lighting. The falls face roughly southeast, so morning light can illuminate the water beautifully. For the fewest crowds, visit early morning or later in the evening. Midday visits are fine but can be crowded in summer.
