The Story of Canada's National Parks

The Cave and Basin is where Canada's remarkable conservation legacy began. In November 1883, three railway workers‚ÄîFrank McCabe and brothers William and Tom McCardell‚Äîstumbled upon a steaming hole in the ground while working on the Canadian Pacific Railway. Lowering themselves through the opening, they discovered a cave containing a pool of hot mineral water.

Word of the discovery spread quickly, and disputes over ownership prompted the federal government to intervene. On November 25, 1885, the government set aside 26 square kilometres around the hot springs as the Banff Hot Springs Reserve‚Äîmaking it Canada's first national park and one of the world's first protected natural areas.

This single act of conservation sparked a movement that now protects millions of hectares of wilderness across Canada. Today, Parks Canada manages over 40 national parks, and the Cave and Basin stands as a monument to that original vision of preserving natural wonders for all generations to enjoy.

A World-Changing Discovery

The Cave and Basin didn't just create Canada's first national park‚Äîit helped inspire the global national parks movement. The idea that nature should be protected for public enjoyment rather than exploited for private gain was revolutionary and continues to shape conservation efforts worldwide.

What to See and Do

The Cave

Walk through a tunnel to enter the original cave discovered in 1883. Stand on the viewing platform above the steaming turquoise pool and imagine the wonder those first explorers must have felt. The cave's interior is lit to showcase the mineral formations and the hole in the ceiling through which the original discoverers descended.

The Basin

Outside the main building, view the historic outdoor basin pool where visitors once bathed in the mineral waters. While swimming is no longer permitted (the area is protected habitat), the pool's brilliant turquoise colour and the steam rising from the surface create a mystical atmosphere.

Interactive Exhibits

The interpretive centre features engaging exhibits that tell the story of the hot springs' discovery, the creation of Canada's national parks, and the ongoing importance of conservation. Hands-on displays are perfect for families with children.

Discovery Trail

A short boardwalk trail leads through the marsh area around the hot springs. Interpretive signs explain the unique ecosystem created by the warm mineral water, including the rare plants and animals that thrive here.

Look for the Banff Springs Snail

The Banff Springs Snail exists only in the hot springs of Banff National Park and nowhere else on Earth. This tiny snail (only 5mm long) requires very specific conditions to survive. Look for them in the warm, mineral-rich waters of the basin‚Äîthey're one of the reasons swimming is no longer permitted.

The Historic Bathhouse

The beautiful 1914 bathhouse building is an architectural treasure in itself. Built in the Rustic style typical of national park architecture, the building features local Rundle stone and timber construction that blends harmoniously with the mountain surroundings.

In its heyday, the bathhouse attracted thousands of visitors who came to "take the waters," believing the mineral springs had healing properties. The elegant interior with its high ceilings and large windows evokes the gracious spa culture of the early 20th century.

Today, the restored building houses the interpretive centre, gift shop, and provides access to the cave. Take time to appreciate the craftsmanship and imagine the elegant bathers of a century ago.

Highlights of Your Visit

The Original Cave

Enter through a tunnel to see the cave where it all began. View the steaming hot spring pool from an elevated platform and see the opening the original discoverers climbed through.

Historic Basin Pool

Admire the brilliant turquoise outdoor pool surrounded by steam. Once a popular bathing spot, it's now protected habitat. The color and atmosphere are mesmerizing.

Interpretive Exhibits

Explore interactive displays about the discovery, the birth of national parks, Indigenous connections to the springs, and modern conservation efforts.

Marsh Boardwalk

Stroll the Discovery Trail through the warm-water marsh. Look for rare plants and wildlife that thrive in this unique thermal ecosystem.

Seasonal Programs

Parks Canada offers special programs throughout the year that enhance the Cave and Basin experience:

  • Guided Tours: Join an interpreter for a deeper understanding of the site's history and significance
  • Indigenous Programs: Learn about the First Nations' connections to the hot springs that span thousands of years
  • Family Activities: Special programs for children including scavenger hunts and hands-on activities
  • Photography Workshops: Occasional workshops on capturing the unique beauty of the site
  • Special Events: The site hosts various events throughout the year‚Äîcheck the Parks Canada website for current offerings

Getting There

The Cave and Basin is located at 311 Cave Avenue, about a 10-minute walk from downtown Banff or a 3-minute drive.

By Foot

From downtown Banff, walk west along the Bow River pathway. The scenic 1.3 km trail passes through forest and follows the river to the Cave and Basin. It's a beautiful walk that takes about 15-20 minutes.

By Car

From Banff Avenue, turn west onto Cave Avenue and follow it to the end. Free parking is available in the lot adjacent to the building.

By Roam Transit

Roam Transit Route 4 stops at the Cave and Basin. This is a convenient option if you're exploring multiple attractions without a car.
