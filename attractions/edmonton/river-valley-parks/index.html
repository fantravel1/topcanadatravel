About Edmonton's River Valley

Stretching along the North Saskatchewan River as it winds through the heart of Edmonton, the River Valley park system is North America's largest urban parkland -- 22 times the size of New York's Central Park. This remarkable green ribbon spans 7,400 hectares and includes over 20 distinct parks connected by 160 kilometres of maintained multi-use trails.

The river valley is Edmonton's defining natural feature and the soul of the city. Where most cities turned their riverfronts into industrial zones, Edmonton preserved its valley as parkland, creating an extraordinary urban wilderness where you can spot deer, coyotes, beavers, and over 250 species of birds without ever leaving the city limits.

From the historic Fort Edmonton footbridge to the stunning glass pyramids of the Muttart Conservatory, from the manicured gardens of Government House to the wild ravines of Mill Creek, the River Valley offers something for every outdoor enthusiast in every season.

Fort Edmonton Footbridge

The stunning Fort Edmonton footbridge, opened in 2021, is a 185-metre suspension bridge spanning the North Saskatchewan River. Its elegant curved design has become an iconic Edmonton landmark and provides spectacular views of the river valley and city skyline.

Seasonal Activities

Spring & Summer

Hiking and trail running on 160 km of paths. Cycling on paved and mountain bike trails. Kayaking, canoeing, and stand-up paddleboarding on the North Saskatchewan River. Picnicking, birdwatching, golf, and outdoor festivals in riverside parks.

Fall

Spectacular fall foliage along the valley slopes. The mix of aspen, poplar, and spruce creates brilliant gold-and-green colour displays. Quieter trails, excellent photography, and the start of aurora viewing season from dark spots in the valley.

Winter

Cross-country skiing on groomed trails throughout the valley. Snowshoeing through forested ravines. Fat biking on packed snow. Outdoor skating at Victoria Park and other rinks. Tobogganing on designated hills. Winter hiking with ice formations along the river.

Year-Round

Dog walking in designated off-leash areas. Nature photography and wildlife viewing. Running and fitness along paved paths. Visiting the Muttart Conservatory glass pyramids. Exploring the valley's many lookout points and viewpoints.

Top Parks in the Valley

  • Hawrelak Park: Edmonton's most popular park with a large lake, paddleboat rentals, and host to the Heritage Festival and other events. Excellent picnic facilities and playground
  • Victoria Park / River Landing: Central park area near the Legislature with skating rink, public art, and river access. Starting point for many valley trails
  • Terwillegar Park: Large natural area with off-leash dog areas, sandy beach, and mountain bike trails. Popular with families and dog owners
  • Gold Bar Park: Quieter east-end park with excellent birdwatching, river access, and connection to the Goldstick Trail system
  • Rundle Park: Family-friendly park with golf course, ball diamonds, and the popular Rundle Park Family Fun Zone (winter)
  • Whitemud Park & Ravine: Dense forested ravine with nature trails, creek crossings, and the Whitemud Equine Centre
  • Louise McKinney Riverfront Park: Downtown riverside park near the Shaw Conference Centre with public art and river views

Trail Tips

Download the Edmonton Trail Guide app or pick up a free trail map at city recreation centres. The valley's trail system is well-signed but vast. Paved multi-use trails are shared between cyclists and pedestrians -- keep right and signal when passing. Bring water and sun protection in summer, and dress in layers in winter.

River Activities

The North Saskatchewan River is a major recreational waterway running through the heart of the valley. Popular river activities include:

  • Kayaking & Canoeing: Several rental companies operate in summer. Paddle from Terwillegar to downtown for a scenic half-day trip
  • Stand-Up Paddleboarding: Popular on calmer stretches. Rentals available at several locations along the river
  • River Floats: Casual float trips on inflatable rafts are a summer tradition. The Terwillegar to Laurier stretch is popular
  • Fishing: The North Saskatchewan supports walleye, northern pike, goldeye, and mountain whitefish. Alberta fishing licence required
  • Dragon Boating: The Edmonton Dragon Boat Festival takes place annually at Louise McKinney Park

River Safety

The North Saskatchewan River has a strong current and cold water temperatures even in summer. Always wear a life jacket for water activities. Check water conditions before heading out and never swim in the river unsupervised. Designated swimming is available at nearby lakes.

Wildlife in the Valley

Edmonton's River Valley is home to a remarkable diversity of wildlife for an urban area:

  • Mammals: White-tailed deer, mule deer, coyotes, beavers, porcupines, snowshoe hares, and red foxes are commonly spotted
  • Birds: Over 250 species including bald eagles, great blue herons, pelicans, hawks, owls, and warblers
  • Amphibians: Wood frogs and Canadian toads breed in valley wetlands each spring
  • Fish: Walleye, northern pike, goldeye, and mountain whitefish inhabit the river

Getting to the River Valley

The River Valley is accessible from dozens of entry points across Edmonton. Some of the most popular access points include:

Central Access

The Legislature Grounds and Victoria Park provide easy access from downtown. Walk down the funicular (free public elevator/stairway) from 100 Street to reach the valley floor from the downtown core.

West Access

Hawrelak Park (Groat Road) and Terwillegar Park (Rabbit Hill Road) offer large parking areas and direct trail access on the west side of the city.

East Access

Gold Bar Park and Rundle Park provide access on the east side. The Capilano footbridge connects to trails on both sides of the river.

By Transit

Several ETS bus routes pass near valley access points. The funicular at 100 Street is steps from downtown transit routes.

Insider Tips

  • Best fall colours: Visit in late September to early October for peak foliage along the valley slopes
  • Sunrise and sunset: The Fort Edmonton footbridge and Groat Bridge offer spectacular sunrise/sunset views over the river
  • Wildlife viewing: Early morning and dusk are the best times to spot deer and coyotes. Bring binoculars for birdwatching
  • Winter trails: Cross-country ski trails are groomed regularly at Goldbar, Hawrelak, and Terwillegar parks
  • Quiet escapes: The ravine trails (Mill Creek, Whitemud) feel far from the city even though they are in the heart of Edmonton
  • Combine with attractions: Fort Edmonton Park, the Muttart Conservatory, and the Edmonton Valley Zoo are all within the valley system
