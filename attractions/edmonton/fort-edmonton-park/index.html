About Fort Edmonton Park

Fort Edmonton Park is Canada's largest living history museum, nestled in the North Saskatchewan River Valley in Edmonton's southwest. Spanning 64 hectares, the park takes visitors on a journey through four distinct time periods in Alberta's history, each brought to life by costumed interpreters who stay in character as you explore their world.

The park centres on a faithful reconstruction of the original Fort Edmonton, the Hudson's Bay Company fur trading post that gave the city its name. From there, visitors progress through streets representing 1885, 1905, and the 1920s, experiencing how Edmonton transformed from a remote fur trade outpost to a booming prairie city.

Following a major $165 million renovation and expansion completed in 2021, the park now features the stunning Indigenous Peoples Experience, enhanced exhibits, and the Midway area with vintage rides. It is a must-visit for anyone interested in Canadian history, family outings, or immersive cultural experiences.

Indigenous Peoples Experience

Added during the 2021 renovation, this powerful exhibit explores the deep history and living cultures of the Indigenous peoples of the Edmonton region. Developed in partnership with local First Nations and Metis communities, it uses immersive storytelling, multimedia, and artifacts to share Indigenous perspectives spanning thousands of years.

The Four Time Periods

1846 Fort Era

Step inside the reconstructed Hudson's Bay Company fur trading fort. Meet costumed interpreters playing fur traders, voyageurs, and Indigenous trading partners. Experience the sights, sounds, and smells of the fur trade era. Watch blacksmithing, canoe-building, and traditional crafts.

1885 Settlement Street

Walk the wooden boardwalks of early Edmonton as settlers arrived from eastern Canada and Europe. Visit the Dominion Land Office, general store, and early churches. Learn about the challenges of homesteading on the prairies and the arrival of the North-West Mounted Police.

1905 Boom Town

Experience Edmonton's transformation when Alberta became a province in 1905. This bustling street features a hotel, bank, newspaper office, and early businesses. The city was growing rapidly with railway connections and the promise of prosperity.

1920s Metropolitan Street

Explore Edmonton in the Roaring Twenties with its jazz age energy. Visit the Capitol Theatre, Chicken Charlie's diner, and the Hotel Selkirk (a real working hotel where you can book overnight stays). Enjoy the adjacent Midway with vintage carnival rides.

Rides & Activities

  • Steam Train: Ride a vintage steam locomotive that loops through the park, connecting the historical areas. The train itself is a piece of living history
  • Midway: Vintage carnival rides including a Ferris wheel, carousel, scrambler, and kiddie rides -- all included in admission
  • Streetcar: Ride a restored vintage streetcar along 1905 Street, just as Edmontonians did over a century ago
  • Pony rides: Available seasonally for young children near the 1885 area
  • Hands-on activities: Try your hand at blacksmithing, fur pressing, gold panning, and other historical crafts with costumed interpreters
  • Hotel Selkirk: Book an overnight stay in the 1920s-themed hotel for a truly immersive experience

Visiting Tips

Start at the 1846 Fort and work your way forward through time. Talk to the costumed interpreters -- they are incredibly knowledgeable and stay in character. Bring comfortable walking shoes as the park covers a lot of ground. The steam train saves walking between areas.

Special Events

Fort Edmonton Park hosts popular seasonal events throughout the year:

  • Canada Day Celebrations (July 1): The park's biggest day with special programming, live music, and fireworks
  • Spooktacular (October): Edmonton's premier Halloween event transforms the park into haunted streets with scare actors, themed mazes, and family-friendly activities during the day
  • Christmas at the Fort: Holiday programming with Victorian Christmas traditions, carolers, and festive decorations across all eras
  • Summer programming: Rotating special events, re-enactments, and themed weekends throughout the summer season

Insider Tips

  • Arrive early: The park opens at 10 AM and is least crowded in the first hour. You will have the fort nearly to yourself
  • Engage with interpreters: Ask questions! The costumed staff are passionate historians who love sharing stories. The interactions make the experience
  • Wear comfortable shoes: The park is spread over 64 hectares with gravel paths and boardwalks
  • Bring sunscreen and water: Much of the park is outdoors with limited shade on hot summer days
  • Plan for weather: The park operates rain or shine. Bring layers as river valley weather can change quickly
  • Eat in the park: Egge's Coulee Cookhouse and Johnson's Cafe offer period-themed dining. Chicken Charlie's is a visitor favourite
  • Book Hotel Selkirk: For a unique experience, stay overnight in the 1920s hotel on the park grounds
  • Weekdays are quieter: Visit Monday through Thursday to avoid weekend crowds, especially during summer

Getting There

Fort Edmonton Park is located in Edmonton's river valley, southwest of downtown.

By Car

Take Whitemud Drive to Fox Drive, then follow signs to the park entrance on 143 Street. Free parking is available in the large lot near the entrance.

By Transit

ETS Route 20 connects the park to South Campus LRT station. Service frequency is limited, so check schedules in advance. Consider driving or using a rideshare service for convenience.

By Foot/Bike

The park is connected to the River Valley trail system. A scenic walk or ride along the river trails from downtown takes approximately 45-60 minutes.
