About the Canadian Museum for Human Rights

The Canadian Museum for Human Rights (CMHR) is a groundbreaking institution that opened in 2014 at The Forks in Winnipeg. It is the first museum in the world dedicated exclusively to the evolution, celebration, and future of human rights. As one of Canada's national museums, it stands as a powerful symbol of the country's commitment to human dignity and justice.

The museum's stunning architecture, designed by American architect Antoine Predock, is itself a journey from darkness to light. The building rises from roots of ancient limestone and basalt, through dramatic alabaster-clad ramps, to the soaring Tower of Hope with its glass "cloud" offering panoramic views of Winnipeg. The design symbolizes humanity's ongoing journey toward greater understanding of human rights.

With 11 immersive galleries spread across 24,000 square metres, the CMHR takes visitors on an emotional and intellectual journey through human rights stories from Canada and around the world. Interactive exhibits, personal testimonies, and multimedia installations make complex issues accessible and deeply moving.

The Canadian Journey Gallery

The museum's signature gallery explores the ongoing struggle for rights and freedoms in Canada, from Indigenous peoples' experiences to the Charter of Rights and Freedoms. It confronts difficult chapters honestly while celebrating the progress made through courage and determination.

The 11 Galleries

What Are Human Rights?

An introductory gallery exploring the universal concept of human rights through diverse cultural perspectives and traditions from around the world.

Indigenous Perspectives

Stories of Indigenous peoples' rights, resilience, and worldviews. Features oral histories, art, and examinations of treaties and reconciliation.

Canadian Journeys

The signature gallery exploring pivotal moments in Canada's human rights history, from residential schools to the fight for equality.

Turning Points for Humanity

Examines five mass atrocities including the Holocaust and the Holodomor, exploring how humanity has responded to the worst violations.

Visitor Tip

The museum can be emotionally intense. Take breaks between galleries at the reflection spaces designed throughout the building. Friday evenings offer extended hours until 9 PM with a quieter atmosphere.

The Architecture

The CMHR building is an architectural masterpiece that has become an iconic part of the Winnipeg skyline. Antoine Predock's design draws on the landscape and spirit of the Canadian prairies, incorporating symbolism at every level.

The exterior features "roots" of locally sourced Tyndall limestone, the same stone found in Manitoba's fossils dating back 450 million years. These roots give way to flowing alabaster ramps that wind upward through the galleries, symbolizing the ascent from darkness toward enlightenment.

At the summit, the Tower of Hope rises 100 metres above ground, wrapped in a luminous glass "cloud." From the top, visitors enjoy 360-degree views of Winnipeg and the surrounding prairies, a reward for the journey through the galleries below.

Getting There

The museum is located at The Forks, one of Winnipeg's most popular destinations, at the junction of the Red and Assiniboine rivers.

By Transit

Winnipeg Transit buses serve The Forks area. Routes 38 and 99 stop nearby. The Forks is also accessible via the Southwest Transitway.

By Car

Ample parking is available at The Forks parkade and surface lots. Expect to pay $4-8 CAD depending on duration. The museum is easily accessible from Main Street and the Provencher Bridge.

Walking

The museum is a pleasant 15-minute walk from the Exchange District and downtown Winnipeg along the river walk.
