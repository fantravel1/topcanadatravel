About the Museum

The Montreal Museum of Fine Arts (MMFA) is not just Canada's largest art museum‚Äîit's one of North America's most comprehensive encyclopedic art institutions. Founded in 1860, the museum has grown to encompass five interconnected pavilions spanning both sides of prestigious Sherbrooke Street.

With over 45,000 works in its permanent collection, the MMFA takes visitors on a journey through human creativity from antiquity to the present day. The collection encompasses Canadian and Quebec art, Indigenous works, Old Masters, Impressionism, contemporary art, decorative arts, and world cultures‚Äîall under one sprawling complex.

The museum's architecture is itself a work of art. The original Beaux-Arts building dates to 1912, while recent additions include the striking Michal and Renata Hornstein Pavilion (2016) and the underground tunnels connecting all five buildings beneath Sherbrooke Street.

Free for Young Visitors

In a groundbreaking initiative, the MMFA offers free permanent collection access to all visitors under 21. The museum believes art should be accessible to young people and aims to cultivate the next generation of art lovers.

The Five Pavilions

Jean-Noel Desmarais Pavilion

International art from antiquity to the 20th century, including European Old Masters, Impressionism, and modern art. Home to works by Rembrandt, Monet, Picasso, and more.

Michal and Renata Hornstein Pavilion

The newest addition, featuring world cultures, Mediterranean archaeology, and rotating exhibitions. Stunning contemporary architecture.

Claire and Marc Bourgie Pavilion

Canadian and Quebec art from early settlement to the present. Indigenous art, the Group of Seven, Jean-Paul Riopelle, and contemporary Quebec artists.

Liliane and David M. Stewart Pavilion

Decorative arts and design from the Middle Ages to today. Furniture, Tiffany glass, ceramics, fashion, and industrial design.

The fifth pavilion, the original 1912 building, serves as the main entrance and houses temporary exhibitions, the gift shop, and administrative offices.

Collection Highlights

  • Canadian Art: The most comprehensive collection of Quebec and Canadian art anywhere, including Jean-Paul Riopelle, Emily Carr, and the Group of Seven
  • Indigenous Art: Significant works from First Nations, Inuit, and Metis artists spanning centuries
  • European Masters: Paintings by Rembrandt, El Greco, Mantegna, Canaletto, and the Dutch Golden Age
  • Impressionism: Works by Monet, Renoir, Cezanne, and other masters of light
  • Decorative Arts: Outstanding Tiffany glass collection, Art Nouveau, Art Deco furniture
  • Contemporary Art: Rotating exhibitions of cutting-edge Canadian and international artists
  • Mediterranean Archaeology: Greek, Roman, and Egyptian antiquities

Audio Guide Tip

The free audio guide (available via the MMFA app) provides expert commentary on major works. It's especially valuable for navigating the vast collection and understanding key pieces in context.

Visiting Tips

  • Start with a plan: The museum is massive. Review the floor plan and prioritize your interests
  • Wednesday nights: Extended hours until 9 PM with a relaxed atmosphere
  • Take breaks: The museum cafe offers excellent food and rest between galleries
  • Temporary exhibitions: Check the website for current special exhibitions, which often draw crowds
  • Guided tours: Free docent-led tours are available at scheduled times
  • Photography: Allowed without flash in permanent collections; some temporary exhibitions prohibit photos
  • Coat check: Free coat and bag check makes exploring more comfortable

Getting There

The museum is located on Sherbrooke Street West in the Golden Square Mile, Montreal's museum district.

By Metro

Take the Green Line to Guy-Concordia station. The museum is a 5-minute walk east along Sherbrooke Street. Alternatively, Peel station (Green Line) is a 10-minute walk.

By Bus

Several bus routes stop directly in front of the museum on Sherbrooke Street, including routes 24 and 138.

By Car

Limited street parking is available on surrounding streets. The museum does not have its own parking garage, but nearby commercial lots charge approximately $15-20 for several hours.

By Bike

BIXI stations are located near both entrances. Secure bike parking is available outside the main pavilion.
