About Old Montreal

Step back in time as you wander the narrow cobblestone streets of Old Montreal (Vieux-Montreal), one of North America's most enchanting historic districts. Founded in 1642 by French colonists who named it Ville-Marie, this neighbourhood is the birthplace of Montreal and preserves over four centuries of architectural heritage.

Walking through Old Montreal feels like being transported to a European city. The streets are lined with 17th, 18th, and 19th-century buildings, horse-drawn carriages clip-clop past outdoor cafes, and street performers entertain visitors in the historic squares. It's the most visited neighbourhood in Quebec, drawing over 11 million visitors annually.

The district stretches from the St. Lawrence River waterfront to Rue Saint-Antoine, encompassing Place Jacques-Cartier, Notre-Dame Basilica, the Old Port, Bonsecours Market, and countless restaurants, boutiques, and museums. Whether you're a history enthusiast, foodie, or romantic at heart, Old Montreal offers an unforgettable experience.

Historic Significance

Old Montreal contains North America's greatest concentration of 17th-19th century buildings. The original fortification walls that once protected the city are traced by today's streets, and many buildings have been continuously occupied for over 200 years.

Must-See Attractions

Place Jacques-Cartier

The heart of Old Montreal, this sloping cobblestone square dates to 1804 and is named after the explorer who claimed Canada for France. Lined with colourful 19th-century buildings, outdoor restaurants, flower vendors, and street performers, it's the perfect place to soak in the atmosphere. The Nelson Column at the top has stood since 1809.

The Old Port (Vieux-Port)

Montreal's historic harbour has been transformed into a 2.5-kilometre recreational waterfront with year-round activities. Stroll the promenade, rent a pedal boat, visit the Montreal Science Centre, or enjoy panoramic views of the St. Lawrence River. In winter, the outdoor skating rink is magical.

Bonsecours Market (Marche Bonsecours)

This stunning neoclassical building with its silver dome was once Canada's main public market and even served briefly as the Parliament of Canada. Today it houses Quebec artisan boutiques selling local crafts, fashion, and souvenirs. The building dates to 1847 and is a National Historic Site.

Notre-Dame-de-Bon-Secours Chapel

Known as the "Sailors' Church," this chapel dates to 1771 (with origins to 1657) and features a museum, archaeological crypt, and rooftop observation deck with stunning harbour views. Model ships hang from the ceiling, offerings from sailors grateful for safe voyages.

Insider Tip

Visit early morning (before 10 AM) or evening for the best photos and smallest crowds. The golden hour light on the historic buildings is particularly beautiful, and you'll find it easier to capture the cobblestone streets without crowds.

Experiences in Old Montreal

Walking Tours

Join a guided walking tour to learn the fascinating history behind the buildings. Ghost tours are popular in the evening, exploring the neighbourhood's spooky legends and haunted sites.

Horse-Drawn Carriage

A romantic way to see Old Montreal. Carriages depart from Place d'Armes and Place Jacques-Cartier, offering 30-60 minute tours through the historic streets.

Culinary Scene

From fine dining in historic buildings to casual bistros and terrace dining, Old Montreal is a food lover's paradise. Try traditional Quebec cuisine like poutine, tourtiere, and smoked meat.

Night Photography

The historic buildings beautifully illuminated at night create a magical atmosphere. The reflection of lights on wet cobblestones after rain is particularly stunning.

Seasonal Highlights

  • Summer (June-August): Outdoor terraces in full swing, street performers, festivals, waterfront activities at the Old Port
  • Fall (September-October): Beautiful autumn colours, fewer tourists, comfortable walking weather
  • Winter (November-March): Festive decorations, outdoor skating at the Old Port, cozy restaurants, magical snow-covered streets
  • Spring (April-May): The neighbourhood comes alive again, cafe terraces reopen, pleasant temperatures for walking

Getting There

Old Montreal is easily accessible by public transit and is best explored on foot once you arrive.

By Metro

Take the Orange Line to Place-d'Armes station (directly at Notre-Dame Basilica) or Champ-de-Mars station (near City Hall and Place Jacques-Cartier). Both stations put you in the heart of the action.

By Bike

BIXI bike-sharing stations are located throughout Old Montreal. The waterfront bike path connects to the citywide cycling network. Many streets are pedestrian-friendly.

By Car

Parking is limited and expensive in Old Montreal. If driving, use the underground parking at the Palais des Congres or along the waterfront. Expect to pay $15-25 for several hours.

On Foot

Old Montreal is about a 15-minute walk from downtown via Rue Saint-Urbain or Boulevard Saint-Laurent. The walk takes you through Chinatown.

Tips for Visiting

  • Wear comfortable shoes: The cobblestone streets are beautiful but uneven. Leave the heels at home
  • Bring layers: The waterfront can be windy, even on warm days
  • Learn basic French: While English is widely spoken, attempting French is appreciated and enhances the experience
  • Reserve restaurants: Popular spots fill up quickly, especially on weekends. Book ahead for dinner
  • Explore side streets: The most charming discoveries are often off the main squares
  • Visit multiple times: Old Montreal has different personalities by day and night
