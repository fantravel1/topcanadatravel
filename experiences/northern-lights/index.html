Frequently Asked Questions

The best time to see Northern Lights in Canada is from mid-August to mid-April, with peak viewing from December to March. The optimal viewing window is between 10 PM and 2 AM when skies are darkest. Solar activity follows an 11-year cycle, with the current cycle peaking around 2024-2025, making this an exceptional time for aurora viewing.

Yellowknife, Northwest Territories is consistently rated the world's best place to see Northern Lights, with over 240 aurora-visible nights per year. Other top locations include Whitehorse (Yukon), Churchill (Manitoba), Jasper National Park (Alberta), and Wood Buffalo National Park. Yellowknife sits directly under the auroral oval and has cold, dry winters with exceptionally clear skies.

Northern Lights tour packages in Canada typically range from $150-300 CAD for a single evening guided tour to $2,000-5,000+ CAD for multi-day packages including accommodation and activities. Yellowknife 3-night packages average $1,500-3,000 CAD per person. Budget options include self-drive trips in Yukon or northern Alberta where you only pay for accommodation and car rental, potentially costing as little as $800 CAD for a multi-night trip.

During strong geomagnetic storms (Kp index 7+), Northern Lights can occasionally be seen from southern Canadian cities like Vancouver, Calgary, Edmonton, Toronto, and Ottawa. However, for reliable viewing, you need to travel north to locations like Yellowknife, Whitehorse, or Churchill. Light pollution in cities significantly reduces visibility, so even during strong storms, you'll get better views by driving 30-60 minutes outside city limits.

Dress in multiple warm layers as temperatures can drop to -30¬∞C to -40¬∞C in winter viewing locations. Essential items include: insulated winter jacket rated to -30¬∞C or below, snow pants, thermal base layers (wool or synthetic), warm hat that covers your ears, balaclava or neck warmer, insulated mittens (not gloves ‚Äî they're warmer), thick wool socks, and insulated winter boots rated to -40¬∞C. Many tour operators provide or rent extreme cold weather gear if you don't have your own.

To photograph Northern Lights: Use a camera with manual settings (DSLR or mirrorless), a wide-angle lens (f/2.8 or wider preferred), and a sturdy tripod. Typical settings: ISO 1600-6400, aperture f/2.8 or lower, shutter speed 5-25 seconds depending on aurora movement. Use manual focus set to infinity. Bring extra batteries (cold drains them quickly) and keep spares warm in your inner pocket. Modern smartphones with night modes can capture auroras, but dedicated cameras produce far superior results.

No aurora tour can guarantee sightings as they depend on solar activity, weather, and clear skies. However, booking multiple nights (3+ recommended) significantly increases your chances. Yellowknife claims a 95% success rate over 3 nights. Most reputable tour operators offer rebooking or partial refunds if conditions prevent viewing. The current solar maximum (2024-2025) means stronger and more frequent aurora activity than usual.
